=== THE FOUR CARDINAL SINS ===

1. OVERCLAIM — number, scope, or causal certainty stronger than the source supports.
2. UNDERCLAIM — softening a finding the evidence actually supports.
3. MISLEADING FRAMING — every fact true but the selection or sequencing distorts the picture.
4. UNVERIFIED DETAIL — any specific number, date, named actor, law section, or quote the dossier cannot trace to a primary source.

SECTION id: tldr (type: tldr)
Bullets:
1. A new Malaysian party in the 12 months before an election is almost never trying to win the country — it is trying to win the negotiation the morning after the count.
2. The prize is pivotal-player status: enough seats to be the necessary coalition partner in a fragmented chamber.
3. Six structural conditions make Malaysia uniquely fertile soil.
4. Sabah is the laboratory; the state has had eight ruling configurations in forty years. Sarawak's GPS is the stable counter-example because Sarawak's RM3-billion-a-year oil sales tax removes the incentive to fragment.
5. Most new parties fail. The exceptions — Bersatu, PKR, GRS — share three ingredients: defector with sitting seats, executive office on offer, pre-negotiated coalition slot.
6. Other democracies block this move with thresholds, public-funding rules, ballot-access barriers, and party-property law. Malaysia has chosen none of these defences.
7. A voter is not the audience for the launch speech.

---

SECTION id: pivotal-player (type: concept)
Body: In a chamber where no single bloc commands a majority, the small party with the right number of seats holds bargaining power wildly out of proportion to its vote share. The formal name is the Shapley-Shubik power index. A 30-seat party in an 80-80-30 parliament does not hold 13 per cent of the power. It holds something closer to a third of it, because it is the necessary partner in any winning coalition.

---

SECTION id: base-rates (type: fact, lens: Political)
Body: Of new federal parties launched between 1988 and 2024, the median first-contest result is roughly 3 per cent of contested seats. Five-year survival sits at around 40 per cent. Within two elections of formation, roughly one in four launches end up in government. That last figure is almost entirely concentrated in launches led by defectors with sitting seats, with an executive office on offer, and a coalition slot pre-negotiated. Personality-only vehicles — IMP in 1951, Pejuang, MUDA's federal contest, GTA — have a historic success rate near zero.

---

SECTION id: launches-timeline (type: timeline)
Events: IMP 1951 (Onn Jaafar, failed) — DAP/Gerakan 1969 (partial) — Semangat 46 1988 (Razaleigh, 8 seats 1990 then reabsorbed 1996) — KeADILan 1999 (Wan Azizah/Anwar, 5 seats GE10, durable) — Bersatu 2016 (Mahathir/Muhyiddin, pivotal GE14) — Amanah 2017 (PAS progressives, junior PH) — Pejuang 2020 (Mahathir, zero seats GE15) — MUDA 2020 (Syed Saddiq, 1 seat) — GRS 2020 (Hajiji Noor umbrella, won Sabah state) — GTA 2022 (Mahathir, wiped).

---

SECTION id: bersatu-2016 (type: case)
Body: Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia founded September 2016, registered after months-long ROS process, within twenty months had toppled the Barisan Nasional government. Founders Mahathir Mohamad and Muhyiddin Yassin — former senior UMNO leaders. Grievance was 1MDB. Coalition slot pre-negotiated with PKR, DAP, Amanah inside Pakatan Harapan.

---

SECTION id: pejuang-2020 (type: case)
Body: Parti Pejuang Tanah Air founded August 2020 after Sheraton Move ejected Mahathir from government. In GE15 November 2022, Pejuang contested 121 seats and won zero. Every candidate forfeited the RM10,000 deposit by polling under 12.5 per cent.

---

SECTION id: reframe-morning-after (type: reframe)
Body: The launch is not about the voter. The negotiation that decides who governs Malaysia happens in the seventy-two hours after the count. A vote for a small new party is a loan of bargaining chips to be spent later, by leadership the voter must trust on judgement.

---

SECTION id: cleavage-theory (type: concept)
Body: In 1967, Seymour Martin Lipset and Stein Rokkan argued that party systems freeze around the major social cleavages of a country at the moment of mass enfranchisement. Malaysian voters are sorted along axes that do not collapse into a single left-right line: Malay-Chinese-Indian, Bumiputera-Bornean-non-Malay, urban-rural, secular-Islamist, peninsular-territorial.

---

SECTION id: six-conditions (type: fact, lens: Political)
Body: First, registration bar administrative not constitutional, under Societies Act 1966. Second, ethnic and territorial cleavages. Third, portable patronage. Fourth, post-electoral coalition formation. Fifth, anti-hopping law closed individual defection but not party-level exits. Sixth, personalist voting.

---

SECTION id: malapportionment (type: diagram)
Body: The 1957 Federal Constitution capped voter-number deviation between constituencies at fifteen per cent. A 1962 amendment removed the cap. Today the largest parliamentary constituency contains roughly nine times as many voters as the smallest. A vote in suburban Selangor carries roughly one-ninth the weight of a vote in Putrajaya or rural Sarawak.

---

SECTION id: anti-hopping-loophole (type: fact, lens: Legal)
Body: The Constitution (Amendment) (No. 3) Act 2022 inserted Article 49A, triggering a casual vacancy when an MP resigns from or is expelled from the party. The law restricts the individual, not the party. A whole party can vote internally to leave a coalition and join another, and its MPs keep their seats.

---

SECTION id: article-49a-text (type: quote)
Source: Federal Constitution of Malaysia, Article 49A, inserted by Act A1648 (2022)
Body: A Member of the House of Representatives shall cease to be a member of the House of Representatives and his seat shall become vacant immediately on a date a casual vacancy is established… on the date that the Member resigns as a member of the political party for which he stood in the election; or on the date that the Member ceases to be a member of, or is expelled from, the political party for which he stood in the election.

---

SECTION id: sabah-volatility (type: fact, lens: Regional)
Body: Sabah has cycled through Berjaya, USNO, PBS, BN-controlled, Musa-era BN, Warisan, GRS as ruling configurations with multiple component-party recombinations. Federal-territorial cleavage anchored in Twenty Points and MA63. Bumiputera-non-Malay composition: Kadazan-Dusun-Murut, Sino-Kadazan, Sulu, Bajau.

---

SECTION id: grs-2020 (type: case)
Body: Gabungan Rakyat Sabah formed weeks immediately before September 2020 Sabah state election. Umbrella for Bersatu Sabah, PAS, STAR, SAPP, and others. Won the state.

---

SECTION id: sarawak-fiscal-lever (type: diagram)
Body: GPS coalition held Sarawak without major fragmentation since exiting BN in 2018. In 2018 Sarawak imposed a five per cent State Sales Tax on petroleum products produced or imported within the state, generating estimated annual revenue in the two-to-four-billion-ringgit range depending on oil prices. This revenue is structurally independent of Putrajaya.

---

SECTION id: three-launches (type: concept)
Body: When a new party appears in the twelve months before a vote, it is doing one of three things. Pivot play: defector with sitting seats. Grievance vehicle: real generational dissatisfaction with no existing party owning it. Vanity option: personality registering a vehicle to retain relevance.

SECTION id: pivotal-from-your-chair (type: concept)
Body: When a voter casts for a small new party expecting policy X, what the voter delivers is bargaining power, not policy. A vote for a pivot party is a vote of confidence in the negotiating judgement of its leadership.

---

SECTION id: view (type: view)
Body: A new-party launch should shift your opinion not on the launcher's claims, but on the system that produced the launch. If this new party did not exist, where would your vote go and why. If the answer is to the same coalition, the launch has not changed the math. You are not the audience for the launch speech. You are the resource the launcher needs to be in a room you cannot see.
