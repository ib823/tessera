[Section: cover] Title: How New Parties Are Built in Malaysia. Subtitle: A Field Guide to the Launch-and-Pivot Playbook. 22-min read.

[Section: tldr] Bullets:
1. A new Malaysian party in the 12 months before an election is almost never trying to win the country — it is trying to win the negotiation the morning after the count.
2. The prize is pivotal-player status: enough seats to be the necessary coalition partner in a fragmented chamber.
3. Six structural conditions make Malaysia uniquely fertile soil: low registration bar, ethnic cleavages, portable patronage, post-electoral coalition formation, the 2022 anti-hopping law's party-level loophole, and personalist politics.
4. Sabah is the laboratory; the state has had eight ruling configurations in forty years. Sarawak's GPS is the stable counter-example — fiscally anchored by petroleum sales-tax revenue of roughly RM3 to RM4 billion a year, in a state assembly where opposition representation has shrunk from six members to two of eighty-two as of April 2024.
5. **POST-v4-FIX:** Most new parties fail. The fastest office-seeking exceptions — Bersatu in 2016 and GRS in 2020 — share three ingredients at launch: sitting-seat leverage, executive office on offer in a friendly coalition, and a pre-negotiated coalition slot. PKR is a different kind of exception: a Reformasi durability vehicle that took multiple election cycles to convert movement politics into institutional power.
6. Other democracies block this move with thresholds, public-funding rules, ballot-access barriers, and party-property law. Malaysia has chosen none of these defences.
7. A voter is not the audience for the launch speech. A voter is the resource the launcher needs to be in a room the voter cannot see. Vote by judging who will be in that room — and push, where you can, for that room to be opened to outside scrutiny.

[Section: pivotal-player] In a chamber where no single bloc commands a majority, the small party with the right number of seats holds bargaining power wildly out of proportion to its seat share. The formal name is the Shapley-Shubik power index. A 30-seat bloc in an 80-80-30 chamber holds about 16 per cent of the seats — and under the Shapley-Shubik index, roughly a third of the bargaining power, because it is the necessary partner in either feasible majority.

[Section: base-rates] **POST-v4-FIX:** The available examples suggest two distinct exception patterns. Across new federal parties launched in Malaysia between 1988 and 2024, the typical first-contest result is a single-digit fraction of the seats they choose to contest. Many do not survive five years; they are deregistered, dormant, or absorbed. The fast office-seeking exceptions — Bersatu in 2016 the clearest case at federal level, GRS in 2020 at state level — reach government within two elections of formation by combining the three structural ingredients above: sitting MPs in the launch party, an executive office on offer in a friendly coalition, and a pre-negotiated slot at the table. A separate exception model is the durability launch, where a movement vehicle survives early electoral marginality and converts to power over multiple cycles — PKR after 1999 is the canonical case, winning five seats at first contest and reaching federal government only nineteen years later through coalition. Personality-only vehicles — IMP in 1951, Mahathir's Pejuang and the GTA umbrella in 2022, MUDA at federal level — have rarely converted to office by either route. The empirical lesson, even on the cases the dossier examines closely, is not that any single move always works. It is that the fast pivot works under three specific conditions, the durability route works under a different set, and personality alone works under neither.

[Section: launches-timeline] IMP 1951 (Onn Jaafar, failed) — **POST-v4-FIX:** DAP and Gerakan: DAP formed 1965, registered March 1966; Gerakan founded March 1968; both broke through electorally in the 1969 general election, breaking MCA monopoly on Chinese-language politics — Semangat 46 1988 (Razaleigh, 8 seats 1990, reabsorbed 1996) — KeADILan 1999 (Wan Azizah Wan Ismail — founded and led Parti Keadilan Nasional through Anwar Ibrahim's incarceration; party president 1999 to 2018; later Malaysia's first female Deputy Prime Minister; 5 seats at first contest; durable spine of PKR) — Amanah 2015 (PAS progressives; durable junior PH partner since GE14) — Bersatu 2016 (Mahathir/Muhyiddin; pivotal GE14) — Pejuang 2020 announced / 2021 registered (Mahathir; zero seats GE15) — MUDA 2020 (Syed Saddiq; 1 seat) — GRS 2020 (Hajiji Noor umbrella; won Sabah state) — GTA 2022 (Mahathir umbrella; wiped GE15).

[Section: bersatu-2016] Parti Pribumi Bersatu Malaysia approved by ROS September 2016; within twenty months toppled BN in GE14 May 2018. Founders Mahathir Mohamad and Muhyiddin Yassin — former senior UMNO leaders with portable patronage. Grievance: 1MDB. Coalition slot pre-negotiated with PKR, DAP, Amanah inside PH umbrella. Personal incentives — cabinet posts, GLC control, ministerial portfolios — distributed to the defectors. Same logic ran through the 2020 Sheraton Move.

[Section: pejuang-2020] Mahathir announced a new party in August 2020 after the Sheraton Move ejected him from government; Parti Pejuang Tanah Air was formally registered in July 2021. In GE15 in November 2022, Mahathir's Gerakan Tanah Air (GTA) umbrella — with Pejuang as principal vehicle — announced 121 parliamentary candidates and contested seats in nearly every state. The bloc won zero seats; Mahathir himself lost his Langkawi seat, and a large share of GTA candidates including the founder forfeited the RM10,000 deposit by polling under one-eighth of the valid votes cast.

[Section: reframe-morning-after] The launch is not about the voter. The voter is the input the launcher needs to reach a table the voter is not at.

[Section: cleavage-theory] Lipset & Rokkan (1967) applied to Malaysian multi-axis cleavages: Malay-Chinese-Indian, Bumiputera-Bornean-non-Malay, urban-rural, secular-Islamist, peninsular-territorial.

[Section: six-conditions] Six structural conditions: Societies Act 1966 registration; ethnic and territorial cleavages; portable patronage; post-electoral coalition formation; the 2022 anti-hopping law's party-level loophole; personalist voting.

[Section: malapportionment] The original 1957 Federal Constitution capped voter-number deviation between constituencies at fifteen per cent. A 1962 amendment repealed that rule and moved apportionment principles into the Thirteenth Schedule. A 1973 amendment removed the quantified national deviation cap entirely from the current text. Today, by the Election Commission's own published voter roll for the GE15 (PRU15) delineation cycle, the largest parliamentary constituency contains roughly ten to eleven times as many voters as the smallest — Bangi at about 303,000, Igan at about 28,000. The UNDI18 reform of 2021 added roughly 5.8 million new voters; the post-UNDI18 roll shows the disparity remains severe rather than narrowing.

[Section: anti-hopping-loophole] The Constitution (Amendment) (No. 3) Act 2022 (Act A1663) inserted Article 49A. Two loopholes survive: expulsion (Article 49A(2)(c) excludes expulsion from the vacancy trigger) and party-level (the law binds the individual, not the party as a corporate body).

[Section: article-49a-text] Source: Federal Constitution of Malaysia, Article 49A, inserted by the Constitution (Amendment) (No. 3) Act 2022 (Act A1663) — received Royal Assent on 31 August 2022 and published in the Gazette on 6 September 2022.

[Section: sabah-volatility] Sabah has cycled through Berjaya, USNO, PBS, BN-controlled, Musa-era BN, Warisan, GRS. The 2014 Royal Commission of Inquiry on illegal immigrants in Sabah documented illegal or irregular issuance of Malaysian identity documents and evidence linking some recipients to the electoral roll. As of late 2021, opposition MPs and civil-society critics reported that the RCI's core recommendations remained largely unimplemented.

[Section: grs-2020] Gabungan Rakyat Sabah was formed in the weeks immediately before the September 2020 Sabah state election as a loose electoral umbrella of Perikatan Nasional, Barisan Nasional and Parti Bersatu Sabah. It won. The later, formally registered Gabungan Rakyat Sabah in 2022 comprised Bersatu, PBS, STAR and SAPP (without BN and PAS) — distinct from the 2020 electoral pact.

[Section: sarawak-fiscal-lever] If Sabah is the laboratory of volatility, Sarawak is the laboratory of stability. In 2018 Sarawak imposed a 5% State Sales Tax on petroleum products. Audited collections in successive state budget speeches ranged from RM2.74 billion in 2020 to RM4.23 billion in 2022. DAP and other opposition representatives have publicly stated that opposition-held constituencies receive lower state development allocations; opposition representation in the eighty-two-seat state assembly has shrunk from six members post-2021 to two as of April 2024.

[Section: three-launches] Pivot play, grievance vehicle, vanity option.

[Section: they-are-all-the-same] Cynicism as lazy abdication.

[Section: this-one-is-different] Naïveté as mark.

[Section: five-questions] Five structural questions: who's inside, who funds, what slot, what job, where in five years.

[Section: pivotal-from-your-chair] Voter loans bargaining chips, not policy purchase.

[Section: view] Vote by judging who will be in the room. B40 reality check: for many Malaysian voters (Felda settlers, plantation workers, kepala kampung), the calculation is "which vote is safe given who already controls my livelihood."
