## ISSUE

On **7 April 2026 in Putrajaya**, National Unity Minister Datuk **Aaron Ago Dagang** announced that the National Unity and National Integration Department (**JPNIN**) had restarted drafting of a **National Harmony and Reconciliation Commission Bill** (Suruhanjaya Keharmonian dan Rekonsiliasi Nasional — SKRN). The minister said the government would draw on three international comparators — **Singapore's Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act 1990, the Australian Human Rights Commission's framework under Racial Discrimination Act s.18C, and Zimbabwe's National Peace and Reconciliation Commission Act** — and that the proposed Malaysian commission would "emphasise preventive and reconciliatory approaches" through **early intervention, mediation and constructive resolution** rather than "purely punitive measures." Stakeholder consultations had begun "early this month" and would continue with government agencies, enforcement bodies, NGOs, political parties and local communities.

This is the **fourth attempt in 14 years** to put a Harmony framework into law: a 2012 announcement under PM Najib that the Sedition Act 1948 would be repealed and replaced; three draft bills produced in 2014–2015 by the National Unity Consultative Council (NUCC); a 2019 "agreed in principle" Cabinet decision on the Commission Bill alone; and an April 2020 reversal that quietly killed it. The Sedition Act remains in force.

## CONTEXT

The choice of comparator countries is not cosmetic — it signals the enforcement model the drafters are considering, and each model has a hard, public track record:

**1. Singapore (Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act 1990, in force 1992; amended 2019; supplemented by Maintenance of Racial Harmony Act 2025 from 7 January 2025).** The MRHA is a coercive statute on its face. Under sections 8 and 9, the Minister for Home Affairs may issue a **restraining order** against any priest, monk, pastor, imam, elder, office-bearer or other person of authority in a religious group who is "causing feelings of enmity, hatred, ill-will or hostility between different religious groups," promoting a political cause under religious guise, or "exciting disaffection against the President or the Government." Restraining orders carry criminal penalty on breach — up to **S$10,000 fine and/or 2 years' jail** for first offence, **S$20,000 and/or 3 years** for repeat. Yet the operationally striking fact is this: **no restraining order has ever been issued under the MRHA**, from 1992 through the most recent parliamentary disclosure on point (Minister for Home Affairs, 12 February 2007 written reply), and no public record indicates one has issued since. The Act functions, in practice, as a credible threat that triggers community-level mediation by the PCRH and the inter-faith Inter-Religious Organisation rather than as a prosecutorial tool. The 2019 amendment (Act 25/2019) added online-content takedown powers and removed the 14-day notice period.

**2. Australia (Racial Discrimination Act 1975, section 18C).** Section 18C makes it **unlawful — not criminal** — to "offend, insult, humiliate or intimidate" a person or group in public because of race, colour, nationality or ethnic origin. Section 18D exempts artistic works, academic and scientific inquiry, and fair comment on matters of public interest done "reasonably and in good faith." Enforcement is by **civil conciliation through the AHRC**: the vast majority of complaints are either conciliated, withdrawn, or dismissed, often ending in an apology. The most cited judicial example is *Eatock v Bolt* [2011] FCA 1103, where the Federal Court found two Andrew Bolt columns breached s.18C and ordered a corrective notice — no criminal sanction. The provision survived a Turnbull-government repeal attempt that was voted down in the Senate on **30 March 2017 by 31 to 28**, after months of debate over replacing "offend, insult, humiliate" with "harass."

**3. Zimbabwe (National Peace and Reconciliation Commission, NPRC).** Established by Constitution of Zimbabwe Amendment Act No. 20 of 2013 and operationalised by NPRC Act Chapter 10:32 of 2018, the NPRC was given a 10-year statutory life-cycle to address "post-conflict justice, healing and reconciliation," with the unresolved Gukurahundi atrocities of 1983–1987 as the central case. Its mandate **ended on 4 January 2023 with substantially no Gukurahundi reconciliation outcome.** Academic and civil-society assessments (Veritas Zimbabwe annual reports, United Zimbabwe Alliance review 2023, Tandfonline African Security Review 2022, Kujenga Amani SSRC 2019) attribute the failure to political interference, lack of independence from ZANU-PF, resource constraints, and absence of political will. The Commission ran Provincial Peace Committees but did not deliver truth-telling, justice or victim redress in any sustained way.

In Malaysian context, the gap between announcement and statute is the central pattern. The Sedition Act 1948 has now survived **two prime ministers** who pledged to repeal it (Najib 2012, Anwar 2018 & 2022). The Penal Code section 298A is on the books but was held unconstitutional outside the Federal Territories in *Mamat bin Daud v Government of Malaysia* [1988] 1 MLJ 119. CMA s.233 was strengthened to RM500,000 penalty in February 2025 then read down by the apex court on 6 February 2026 (T4A issue 1980). Into this layered legal field, the SKRN Bill is being drafted **without repeal of any of the existing punitive statutes**.

## CONTRADICTIONS

1. **Sedition cases 2024 baseline.** CIJ press release (December 2025) and the Star focus piece (15 December 2025) both reference the 36 cases / 84% rise figure for 2025; CIJ cites the 2024 baseline as 19. The Star focus piece does not name the 2024 figure directly. T4A issue 1980 uses 36 / 19 / 84%. **Authoritative source: CIJ Malaysia 2025 annual review.** Use 19 → 36, +84%, attributed to CIJ.

2. **"Three bills" in 2014 vs "two rejected, one in principle."** Aaron Ago Dagang's 7 April 2026 statement (FMT/Bernama) says **three drafts** (NHRB, NHRCB, RRHCB) were prepared in 2015, two were rejected, NHRCB was "agreed in principle" in 2019. The Malaysian Insight historical reporting (Joseph Kurup, 2017) describes "three unity bills." The Nut Graph contemporaneous 2014 coverage refers to the three. **Authoritative: NUCC drafting record and Aaron's 2026 statement aligned.** Use "three drafts in 2014–2015; one survived to 2019; none enacted as of May 2026."

3. **MRHA restraining order count.** Wikipedia's MRHA article (citing the 12 February 2007 Singapore Parliament written reply by the Minister for Home Affairs) records **zero restraining orders ever issued**; no public source surfaced through 2024 indicating any have since been issued. **The 2026 Maintenance of Racial Harmony Act regime is separate** and not yet activated as far as the search reveals. Be careful: T4A's card should say "**no Section 8 or 9 restraining order has been publicly recorded** under the MRHA since it came into force in 1992" — softer phrasing matches the evidence (we have parliamentary disclosure to 2007 + absence of contradicting record since, not affirmative 2024 disclosure).

4. **Zimbabwe NPRC end-date.** Multiple sources put the statutory term at "10 years from constitutional establishment 2013," ending 2023; Newsday and other outlets describe the term ending in early 2023; NPRC's own annual report 2023 was filed for the final calendar year of operation. **Use: "10-year statutory mandate ended in 2023."**

5. **Aaron Ago Dagang's portfolio history.** Wikipedia gives Aaron as Minister of National Unity since December 2022 in the Anwar cabinet. He previously served as Deputy Minister of Health II under PN (Muhyiddin, 2020). **Use: "Minister of National Unity since December 2022."**

## SUMMARY for editorial review

Three threads, one issue:

1. **The pattern**: This is the fourth attempt in 14 years to put a Harmony framework into Malaysian law. Two PMs have promised repeal of the Sedition Act and broken the promise.
