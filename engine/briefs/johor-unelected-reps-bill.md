# ISSUE 1977 — Johor Unelected Reps Bill

## ISSUE
On 7 May 2026, the Johor State Legislative Assembly passed a Bill amending the *Undang-undang Tubuh Negeri Johor 1895* to allow the appointment of up to five non-elected assemblymen, raising the maximum size of the assembly from 56 to 61. The amendment cleared the two-thirds bar by exactly the margin Barisan Nasional (BN) holds from the 2022 state election: 40 in favour, 16 against. Menteri Besar Datuk Onn Hafiz Ghazi tabled the Bill and framed it as broadening "policy input"; Pakatan Harapan, Muda, the electoral-reform group Tindak Malaysia, and DAP deputy chairman Nga Kor Ming objected on democratic-mandate and Federal Constitution grounds. Onn Hafiz also explicitly ruled out an immediate state election. The next Johor state election is constitutionally due by March 2027.

## PERIOD
4 May 2026 (proposal surfaces in state press via Yong Peng rep) — 10 May 2026 (most recent reporting in dataset). Immediate window: 4–10 May 2026. Historical context: 1895 (original UUTKNJ), 2022 Johor state election (current 56-seat composition), Pahang 2020/2024 and Sabah 2025 precedents.

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **14 April 1895**: Sultan Abu Bakar of Johor, with 17 nobles, promulgates the *Undang-undang Tubuh Negeri Johor* — the first written constitution in any Malay state. After the 1946 anti-Malayan-Union protest, other Malay states adopt it as a model. (Wikipedia: Johor State Constitution; Tandfonline 2013, "Re-reading the Constitution and Majalah in 1890s Johor")
- **November 2020**: Pahang State Assembly passes a constitutional amendment raising the maximum size of its assembly from 42 to 47 by allowing up to five appointed members. (Malay Mail, 20 Nov 2020)
- **12 March 2022**: Johor 15th state election. BN wins 40 of 56 seats (a two-thirds majority by exactly one seat); PH 11; PN 3; PKR 1; MUDA 1. (Malay Mail official tally, 12 Mar 2022)
- **15 March 2022**: Onn Hafiz Ghazi (UMNO, BN) sworn in as Menteri Besar of Johor. (The Malaysian Reserve, 16 Mar 2022)
- **13 May 2024**: Pahang appoints its first five non-elected assemblymen under the 2020 amendment — a UMNO division chief, an MCA division chief, an Amanah state chairman, a PKR deputy state chairman, and a *tahfiz* school founder. The appointed reps can vote, debate, and sit on committees, but cannot become MB or join the State Executive Council. (Malay Mail; The Star; FMT, 13 May 2024)
- **December 2025**: Sabah's GRS-led government, after the November 2025 state election, appoints six nominated assemblymen, bringing the assembly's effective size to 79 (73 elected + 6 nominated). (FMT, 1 Dec 2025; The Star, 11 Dec 2025)
- **4 May 2026**: Johor DAP rep (Yong Peng) publicly questions a proposal — first surfaced internally — to introduce five appointed Johor assemblymen. (FMT, 4 May 2026)
- **5 May 2026**: BN-aligned Yong Peng rep confirms the state government will table the proposal at the 7 May sitting. (The Star, 5 May 2026)
- **6 May 2026**: Muda calls on Johor reps to vote against the Bill; Tindak Malaysia, Projek Sama, and Johor PKR publicly object. (Malay Mail; FMT, 6 May 2026)
- **7 May 2026 (Thursday, Kota Iskandar)**: MB Onn Hafiz tables the constitutional amendment. The Bill amends Article 15 of Part II of the *Undang-undang Tubuh Negeri Johor 1895* by inserting the words *"not exceeding five appointed members"* after *"fifty-six"*, and adds new Clauses 15A, 15B, and 15C governing the appointment process. The Bill passes with 40 votes in favour, 16 against. The MB rules out an immediate snap state election. (The Star; Malay Mail; The Vibes; FMT; The Vibes — all 7 May 2026)
- **9 May 2026**: DAP deputy chairman Nga Kor Ming publicly calls the Johor amendment unconstitutional, arguing it contravenes the Federal Constitution's requirement that state assemblymen be elected, and describes appointment as a "backdoor" route into office. He also argues appointed reps should not have the same voting powers as elected reps. (FMT; The Star; NST, 9 May 2026)
- **9–10 May 2026**: BN leaders (Johor UMNO Youth; MCA president Wee Ka Siong) accuse DAP of "double standards," noting that DAP did not block similar amendments in Pahang or Sabah. (The Star, 9–10 May 2026; The Vibes, 10 May 2026; Malaysiakini, 10 May 2026)

## ACTORS

**State government:**
- **Datuk Onn Hafiz Ghazi** — Menteri Besar of Johor (UMNO, BN). Tabled the amendment Bill 7 May 2026. Stated rationale: broaden assembly's "expertise and experience" given Johor's economic growth (RTS Link, JS-SEZ). Ruled out a snap state election.
- **Johor State Legislative Assembly (14th term)** — 56 elected ADUN. Composition from 2022 election: BN 40, PH 11, PN 3, PKR 1, MUDA 1.
- **Sultan Ibrahim Sultan Iskandar** — Sultan of Johor and concurrently the 17th Yang di-Pertuan Agong of Malaysia (since 31 Jan 2024). Royal assent is required for any appointment under the new Clauses 15A–15C. As of 10 May 2026, no public statement from Istana Pasir Pelangi on the amendment has been reported in the radar dataset.

**Opposition / civil society:**
- **Nga Kor Ming** — DAP deputy chairman; federal Housing & Local Government Minister. The most prominent voice arguing the amendment is unconstitutional.
- **Pakatan Harapan Johor** — calls the Bill "rushed" and prefers electoral redelineation as the legitimate route to broader representation. Has begun a signature campaign.
- **Muda** — calls on all Johor assemblymen, including BN backbenchers, to reject the Bill.
- **Tindak Malaysia** (director Danesh Prakash Chacko) — argues the amendment "artificially inflates the government's majority" and creates a "self-serving" system controlled by the ruling coalition.
- **Projek Sama, Johor PKR** — argue allowing unelected reps to vote on state matters dilutes the elected mandate.

**BN counter-voices:**
- **Wee Ka Siong** — MCA president. Argues DAP is "consistently inconsistent" — it raised no equivalent objection to Pahang (2020/2024) or Sabah (2025) appointments.
- **Johor UMNO Youth** — argues non-elected reps "make for stronger governance" by injecting expertise.

**Comparators (precedent states):**
- **Pahang** — 5 appointed since May 2024 under 2020 amendment (Ahmad Irshad Abdullah Al-Qardi, Wong Tat Chee, Haris Salleh Hamzah, Mohd Fadzli Mohd Ramly, Rizal Jamin). All five are party officials or community leaders, not non-aligned technocrats.
- **Sabah** — 6 nominated since Dec 2025 (Mohamed Razali Mohamed Razi, Ceasar Mandela Malakun, Abdul Kassim Razali, Roger Chin, Chin Shu Ying, Grace Lee Li Mei).
- **Terengganu** — up to 4 appointed (introduced 1999 under PAS MB Hadi Awang; capped at women and non-Muslim representatives per the 1999 enactment).

## RELEVANT LAW

1. **Undang-undang Tubuh Negeri Johor 1895** (Johor State Constitution)
   - **Article 15, Part II**: Sets the size of the Legislative Assembly. Pre-amendment text: "fifty-six" elected members. Post-amendment text: "fifty-six" + "not exceeding five appointed members" = up to 61 total.
   - **New Clauses 15A, 15B, 15C** (introduced 7 May 2026): Procedure for appointment — motion supported by at least 7 assemblymen → simple majority approval in the assembly → consent of the Sultan of Johor.
2. **Federal Constitution of Malaysia**
   - **Article 71**: Federal guarantee of state constitutions and rulers. Federal Parliament may amend a state constitution that lacks the "essential provisions" set out in the Eighth Schedule, or contains provisions inconsistent with them.
   - **Eighth Schedule, Section 1 ("essential provisions")**: Each state constitution must establish a Legislative Assembly *consisting of the Ruler and "such number of elected members as may be provided by State law"* — this is the textual basis on which Nga Kor Ming's "must be elected" argument rests. The schedule does not, on its face, prohibit additional non-elected members; the constitutional question is whether they impair the "essential" elected character. The question has not been tested in the Federal Court.
   - **Eighth Schedule, Section 19(2)**: No provision of any state constitution relating to royal succession or the position of Malay Ruling Chiefs may be amended by a state assembly.
   - **1994 amendment to Eighth Schedule**: Ruler must assent within 30 days, or the Bill becomes law without assent.
3. **Pahang State Constitution (precedent)** — 2020 amendment created equivalent appointed-member provisions; not constitutionally challenged in court.
4. **Sabah State Constitution (precedent)** — provides for up to 6 nominated members under Article 14.
5. **Terengganu State Constitution (precedent)** — 1999 amendment under MB Hadi Awang created up to 4 appointed seats, restricted to women and non-Muslim representatives.

## KEY STATISTICS

- **40** — assemblymen voting in favour of the Bill on 7 May 2026 (BN holds exactly 40 seats from the 2022 election; vote tracks the BN/non-BN split). Source: Malay Mail; The Star, 7 May 2026.
- **16** — assemblymen voting against (PH 11 + PN 3 + PKR 1 + MUDA 1 = 16). Sources as above.
- **2/3** — constitutional threshold for amending the *Undang-undang Tubuh Negeri Johor*; equals 38 of 56. BN's 40-seat majority cleared this by exactly 2 votes.
- **5** — maximum number of appointed assemblymen permitted by the new Clauses 15A–15C.
- **61** — new maximum size of the Johor State Assembly (56 elected + 5 appointed).
- **7** — minimum number of assemblymen required to support a motion to appoint a non-elected member, per Clause 15B (lower than the 19 ADUN that PH+PN+PKR+MUDA collectively command).
- **131 years** — between the 1895 promulgation of UUTKNJ and the 2026 amendment.
- **4** — number of Malaysian states (after Johor) with appointed-member provisions: Sabah, Terengganu, Pahang, Johor.
- **5/5** — Pahang's appointed reps (May 2024) who were either ruling-coalition party officials (UMNO division chief, MCA division chief, Amanah state chairman, PKR deputy state chairman) or aligned community figures. None were non-aligned independents.
- **March 2027** — constitutional deadline for the next Johor state election (5-year term from March 2022 swearing-in).

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **Vote count framing**: The Star says "two-thirds vote involving 40 of the 56 assemblymen" (read literally: 40 yes, 16 no, 0 abstain). The Vibes says "more than two-thirds support, with 40 assemblymen in favour and 16 opposing it" (same numbers, explicit). Malay Mail says "40 assemblymen voting for it and the remainder against." **No real contradiction; we use 40-16.** The interesting gap is that 40/56 = 71.4%, not 66.7% — BN cleared the bar by exactly 2 votes above threshold.
- **Number of appointed reps in Sabah**: search results report 6 appointed (consistent across The Star 11 Dec 2025 and FMT 1 Dec 2025). One Wikipedia overview describes the schema as "up to 6 nominated members"; we use "6 appointed since Dec 2025" with "up to 6" as the constitutional ceiling.
- **Onn Hafiz's stated rationale**: Different outlets summarise this as "broaden policy input" (Malay Mail), "expertise and experience" (The Star), "strengthen institutional capacity amid economic development" (other). All three are paraphrases of the same MB speech; no contradiction. We will quote the most concrete: "expertise and experience to strengthen the quality of debates and the policymaking process."
- **Constitutional argument**: Nga Kor Ming says appointed reps contravene the Federal Constitution's requirement that assemblymen "must be elected." The Eighth Schedule Section 1 text references "such number of elected members as may be provided by State law" — this does not, on its face, *prohibit* additional non-elected members. **The constitutional question is genuine but textually contested and untested in court.** We must report this as a contested legal claim, not as established law in either direction.
- **DAP "double standards" claim**: Wee Ka Siong / Johor UMNO Youth argue DAP did not block Pahang (2020) or Sabah (Dec 2025). At the federal level, DAP was in opposition during Pahang's 2020 amendment and in coalition with the federal government during Sabah's Dec 2025 appointments. We will report the bare structural facts and let readers judge consistency.
