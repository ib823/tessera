# ISSUE 1988 — Court of Appeal: Emergency Proclamations Beyond Judicial Review

## ISSUE

On 4 May 2026 a three-judge panel of the Malaysian Court of Appeal dismissed lawyer Syed Iskandar Syed Jaafar's appeal seeking leave to judicially review the Yang di-Pertuan Agong's October 2020 refusal to issue a Proclamation of Emergency at then-Prime Minister Muhyiddin Yassin's request. Justice Collin Lawrence Sequerah, sitting with Justices Supang Lian and Alwi Abdul Wahab, held that Article 150(8) of the Federal Constitution — the ouster clause inserted by the 1981 constitutional amendment — was deliberately framed to remove the courts' jurisdiction over the Agong's "satisfaction" that a grave emergency exists, and that "policy-centric matters such as considerations pertaining to the security of the federation, friendly relations with other countries, public order, and morality" are beyond the institutional competence of the judiciary. The court allowed the appeal to proceed on public-interest grounds despite the academic nature of the relief sought, but ruled on the substantive question against the appellant.

The decision is structurally significant beyond its procedural outcome. It re-affirms the Article 150(8) ouster against the line of cases — most prominently *Indira Gandhi v Pengarah JAIP* [2018] 1 MLJ 545 — that have held judicial review to be part of the Constitution's basic structure and incapable of being abrogated by Parliament. The 2026 panel acknowledged the basic structure doctrine but treated emergency proclamations as a category that 150(8) had deliberately carved out of it. The ruling thus locks in the 1981 amendment's reversal of the *Teh Cheng Poh v Public Prosecutor* [1979] 1 MLJ 50 Privy Council position that emergency proclamations were reviewable.

## PERIOD

23 October 2020 (Muhyiddin's first emergency request to the Agong) — 4 May 2026 (Court of Appeal ruling). Forward-looking: potential Federal Court leave application from Syed Iskandar; no appeal route yet confirmed publicly.

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **23 October 2020**: PM Muhyiddin Yassin meets Yang di-Pertuan Agong Al-Sultan Abdullah Ri'ayatuddin Al-Mustafa Billah Shah at the monarch's residence in Pahang. Earlier the same day Muhyiddin convened a special Cabinet meeting to discuss requesting a Proclamation of Emergency. (Malay Mail, 25 Oct 2020; BenarNews, 25 Oct 2020)
- **24 October 2020**: Former Finance Minister Tengku Razaleigh Hamzah publicly calls Muhyiddin's request "wrong in principle" — characterising it as a move to insulate the PM's slim majority rather than a genuine public-health measure. (Malay Mail, 24 Oct 2020)
- **25 October 2020**: Sultan Abdullah convenes a three-hour meeting at Istana Negara with seven other Malay Rulers — the Sultans of Johor, Perak, Kedah, Terengganu, Negeri Sembilan, Perlis and Selangor — to discuss the request. After the meeting the Istana issues a statement: "there is no need right now for His Royal Highness to declare a state of emergency for the country or any other parts of Malaysia." The Agong expresses satisfaction with the government's COVID-19 handling. (Malay Mail, 25 Oct 2020; BenarNews, 25 Oct 2020)
- **30 October 2020**: Lawyer Syed Iskandar Syed Jaafar files an originating summons at the Kuala Lumpur High Court seeking declarations clarifying the constitutional propriety of the Agong's refusal to act on PM advice under Article 150(1). (Malay Mail, 30 Oct 2020; The Edge, Oct 2020)
- **12 January 2021**: Sultan Abdullah issues the 2021 Proclamation of Emergency on Muhyiddin's renewed request. Parliament is suspended for the first time since 1969 — the longest suspension since independence. Emergency runs nationwide until 1 August 2021 (Sarawak emergency extended past August to delay the 2022 state election). (PMO speech text, 12 Jan 2021; Time, 12 Jan 2021; Wikipedia: 2021 Malaysian state of emergency)
- **11 March 2021**: In *Khairuddin Abu Hassan*, the High Court rules that Article 150(8) shuts the courts' door on any challenge to the 2021 Proclamation or to the emergency ordinances enacted under it. (The Malaysian Reserve, 11 Mar 2021; Malaysia Today, 11 Mar 2021)
- **15 February 2024**: High Court (Justice not named in available reporting) dismisses Syed Iskandar's originating summons at first instance, ruling that the Agong's power to issue or refuse to issue a Proclamation under Article 150(1) is not justiciable in light of Article 150(8). (Malay Mail, 15 Feb 2024; Borneo Post Online, 15 Feb 2024)
- **26 June 2025**: At Court of Appeal hearing, counsel for Syed Iskandar argues that Article 150(8) is itself unconstitutional for offending the basic structure doctrine recognised in *Indira Gandhi* (2018). (FMT, 26 Jun 2025)
- **19 November 2025**: Three-judge Court of Appeal panel reserves judgment after a full hearing. (FMT, 19 Nov 2025)
- **4 May 2026 (anchor date)**: Panel delivers judgment dismissing the appeal. Justice Sequerah authors. Holds Article 150(8) ousts jurisdiction; doctrine of basic structure acknowledged but emergency-proclamation review carved out as a "policy-centric" matter beyond judicial competence; "Salem exception" used to justify ruling on the substantive question despite the academic relief. (FMT, 4 May 2026; The Star, 4 May 2026)

## RELEVANT LAW

1. **Federal Constitution Article 150** — Emergency powers.
   - **Article 150(1)**: "If the Yang di-Pertuan Agong is satisfied that a grave emergency exists whereby the security, or the economic life, or public order in the Federation or any part thereof is threatened, he may issue a Proclamation of Emergency."
   - **Article 150(2B)**: empowers the Agong to promulgate ordinances during emergency.
   - **Article 150(6)**: ousts judicial review of emergency *ordinances* on enumerated grounds.
   - **Article 150(8)** — **inserted by Constitution (Amendment) Act 1981** — the central provision in the 4 May 2026 ruling: "Notwithstanding anything in this Constitution, the satisfaction of the Yang di-Pertuan Agong mentioned in Clause (1) and Clause (2B) shall be final and conclusive and shall not be challenged or called in question in any court on any ground."
2. **Federal Constitution Article 4(1)** — "This Constitution is the supreme law of the Federation and any law passed after Merdeka Day which is inconsistent with this Constitution shall, to the extent of the inconsistency, be void." The basic-structure argument rests on whether 150(8), as an *amendment*, can validly displace 4(1)'s supremacy over judicial review.
3. **Federal Constitution Article 121** — Judicial power vested in the High Courts. In *Indira Gandhi* (2018) the Federal Court held that judicial review is inherent in this vesting and cannot be ousted by Parliament.
4. **Constitution (Amendment) Act 1981** — inserted Article 150(8). Adopted shortly after the Privy Council's 1979 *Teh Cheng Poh* decision had affirmed reviewability of emergency proclamations. The legislative purpose of 150(8) was to reverse *Teh Cheng Poh* and entrench the *Stephen Kalong Ningkan* (1966) majority position.
5. **Constitution (Amendment) Act 1983 / repeal 1984** — Mahathir's attempt to amend Article 150(1) to oblige the Agong to issue a Proclamation when the PM was satisfied that a grave emergency existed. Repealed in 1984 after the Conference of Rulers mounted strong opposition.

## KEY CASES

- ***Stephen Kalong Ningkan v Government of Malaysia*** [1968] 1 MLJ 119 (FC); affirmed on other grounds by Privy Council [1968] AC 379. Federal Court majority (Barakbah LP, Azmi CJM) held that the legality of a Proclamation of Emergency was "wholly beyond the courts' province." Ong Hock Thye FCJ dissented. The Privy Council left the justiciability question explicitly open: "whether a Proclamation under statutory powers by the Supreme Head of the Federation can be challenged before the courts on some or any grounds is a constitutional question of far-reaching importance which, on the present state of the authorities, remains unsettled and debatable."
- ***Teh Cheng Poh alias Char Mer v Public Prosecutor*** [1979] 1 MLJ 50 (PC); [1979] 2 WLR 623. Privy Council held that emergency proclamations and Cabinet decisions announced by the Agong are reviewable. The 1981 amendment inserting Article 150(8) was Parliament's direct legislative reversal of this position.
- ***Indira Gandhi Mutho v Pengarah Jabatan Agama Islam Perak & Ors*** [2018] 1 MLJ 545 (FC). Federal Court held that "the power of judicial review is essential to the constitutional role of the courts, and inherent in the basic structure of the Constitution," and that this cannot be abrogated by constitutional amendment. The 2026 Court of Appeal accepted *Indira Gandhi* in principle but distinguished emergency proclamations as a "policy-centric" category outside the doctrine's reach.
- ***Khairuddin Abu Hassan v Government of Malaysia*** (High Court, 11 March 2021). Application to challenge the 12 January 2021 Proclamation and the Emergency (Essential Powers) Ordinance 2021 dismissed on the basis that Article 150(8) shut the courts' door.
- The "**Salem exception**" — relied on by Justice Sequerah to justify deciding the substantive issue notwithstanding the academic nature of the appellant's requested relief. Originates in *R v Secretary of State for the Home Department, ex parte Salem* [1999] 1 AC 450 (HL): a court has discretion to entertain an academic case where the issue is of public importance and a decision will not be unfair to the party against whom it is given.

### 3R SPECIAL CAUTION (Royalty)

The story turns on the constitutional role of the Yang di-Pertuan Agong and the Conference of Rulers. We must report the Agong's October 2020 refusal as a constitutional act in the *exercise* of the discretion preserved by Article 150 — not as a political judgement, even though the political-context coverage at the time treated it that way. We will use the verb "refused to issue" or "did not accede to" rather than "rejected." We will not characterise the rulers' motivations. We will report the 1983 attempt to remove the Agong's discretion and its 1984 repeal as a matter of constitutional record, citing the Conference of Rulers' opposition as the historical fact it is.
