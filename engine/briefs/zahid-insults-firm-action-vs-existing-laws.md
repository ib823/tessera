# ISSUE 1980 — Zahid's "firm action" call vs. the existing-laws picture

## ISSUE
On 10 May 2026, at the Musyawarah Nasional forum themed "Malay Unease: What Is the Solution?" at the Premieria Hotel in Kuala Lumpur, Deputy Prime Minister Datuk Seri Ahmad Zahid Hamidi — who is also the forum's founder, UMNO president, and Rural & Regional Development Minister — called for "firm, fair and wise" action against insults to Islam and to the institution of the Malay Rulers, citing examples including insults to "Allah", jokes mocking halal matters, objections to the azan, and disrespect toward the Rulers. He said freedom of speech "cannot be used as a pretext" for these acts and that it is time to "review and strengthen" the existing legal framework.

The headline picture mainstream coverage presents is "DPM warns of unease, calls for tougher action." The picture the public record actually supports is more complicated: charges under the Sedition Act hit a five-year high in 2025; investigations under restrictive expression laws rose roughly 23% year-on-year (2024→2025); the main statute prosecutors lean on for online "insult" cases — Section 233(1)(a) of the Communications and Multimedia Act 1998 — had its core words "offensive" and "annoy" struck down by the Court of Appeal in August 2025 (currently on Federal Court appeal); and the bill that has been promised since 2012 as the mediation-based replacement — the National Harmony & Reconciliation Bill — is still in cabinet draft as of April 2026.

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **1948**: Sedition Act enacted by the colonial administration. Section 3(1)(f) makes it seditious to question matters protected by Articles 152, 153, and 181 of the Federal Constitution (the position of Malay Rulers among them). (Sedition Act 1948 — Federal Gazette)
- **1976**: Penal Code amended to insert **Section 298A** (causing disharmony, disunity or feelings of enmity on grounds of religion); Section 298 already criminalised wounding of religious feelings.
- **1998**: Communications and Multimedia Act passed. **Section 233(1)(a)** criminalises any communication "obscene, indecent, false, menacing or **offensive** in character with intent to **annoy**, abuse, threaten or harass." Penalty: up to RM50,000 fine and/or 1 year jail. (CMA 1998 — Federal Gazette)
- **12 July 2012**: PM Najib Razak announces the Sedition Act will be repealed and replaced by a National Harmony Act. (Bernama / MySinchew, 12 July 2012)
- **2014–2015**: Cabinet keeps the Sedition Act; 2015 amendments expand it to cover online expression. The promised National Harmony Bill is never tabled.
- **2020**: Activist Heidy Quah Gaik Li shares a Facebook post about alleged mistreatment of refugees at immigration detention centres during the COVID-19 pandemic; charged under CMA s.233.
- **2023**: Quah's constitutional challenge to the words "offensive" and "annoy" in s.233(1)(a) dismissed by the High Court.
- **April 2025**: SUARAM publishes its annual Malaysia Human Rights Report 2025; documents a **five-year high in charges under the Sedition Act** and continued targeting of journalists and activists under both the Sedition Act and CMA s.233. (SUARAM, April 2025)
- **Calendar year 2025 (CIJ tally)**: Centre for Independent Journalism records **233 investigations and arrests** under laws restricting freedom of expression — up from 189 in 2024, a **23.28% year-on-year increase**. (CIJ, 2025)
- **6 March 2025**: IGP confirms police probe into viral video allegedly insulting Islam. (Malay Mail, 6 March 2025)
- **19 August 2025**: Court of Appeal in *Heidy Quah Gaik Li v Kerajaan Malaysia* unanimously rules that the words "offensive" and "annoy" in s.233(1)(a) of the CMA are unconstitutional and void, violating Articles 8 (equality) and 10 (freedom of speech) of the Federal Constitution. The remainder of s.233 — including "obscene, indecent, false, menacing" — survives. (Amnesty Malaysia, 19 Aug 2025; Rajah & Tann viewpoint; Lexology summary)
- **13 November 2025**: Federal Court grants the government leave to appeal the Court of Appeal's *Heidy Quah* ruling. The Federal Court appeal has not yet been heard as of this brief. (The Star; Malay Mail, 13 Nov 2025)
- **April 2026**: Unity Minister Datuk Aaron Dagang confirms the cabinet is again studying the National Harmony & Reconciliation Bill and a parallel Suruhanjaya Keharmonian dan Rekonsiliasi Nasional (SKRN) Bill, framed as "mediation over punishment" with Singapore, Australia and Zimbabwe cited as comparator jurisdictions. (Utusan Malaysia; Malaysia Tribune, April 2026)
- **26 April 2026**: Selangor police place the suspect in the Ampang azan-petition case on a Special Circular List, barred from leaving the country. Investigation papers cite **Section 4(1) of the Sedition Act** and **Section 233 of the CMA**. (Malay Mail, 26 April 2026)
- **10 May 2026 (Saturday, Premieria Hotel, Kuala Lumpur)**: Musyawarah Nasional event themed "Malay Unease: What Is the Solution?" Zahid, in his capacity as founder of the forum and DPM, delivers the keynote. Says insults to Islam, "Allah", halal matters, the azan and the Malay Rulers must be handled "firmly, fairly and wisely." Announces a secretariat will be formed to gather resolutions, that he personally will ensure they reach the cabinet, and that Musyawarah Nasional will be registered as an NGO. (UMNO Online; BN Comms; Malay Mail; The Star; The Edge; FMT; Malaysiakini — all 10 May 2026)
- **11 May 2026**: PAS president Datuk Seri Hadi Awang publicly responds; says "DNA of betrayal" lies in UMNO, not PAS. (FMT, 11 May 2026)

## RELEVANT LAW

1. **Sedition Act 1948**
   - **Section 3(1)(f)**: An act, speech, words, publication or other thing has a "seditious tendency" if it questions any matter, right, status, position, privilege, sovereignty or prerogative established or protected by the provisions of Part III or Articles 152, 153 or 181 of the Federal Constitution. **Article 181** protects the sovereignty of the Rulers.
   - **Section 4(1)**: Penalty up to RM5,000 fine or 3 years' jail, or both, for a first offence.
   - **2015 amendments**: extended the Act to cover electronic publication and made certain offences non-bailable.

2. **Communications and Multimedia Act 1998**
   - **Section 233(1)(a)**: Pre-*Heidy Quah* version criminalised content "obscene, indecent, false, menacing or **offensive** in character with intent to **annoy**, abuse, threaten or harass." Penalty up to RM50,000 fine, 1 year jail, or both. Post-*Heidy Quah* (Court of Appeal, 19 Aug 2025): the words "offensive" and "annoy" are excised as unconstitutional. The remainder ("obscene, indecent, false, menacing … abuse, threaten or harass") survives.
   - **Federal Court appeal pending** since 13 Nov 2025. Outcome will determine whether s.233 is restored or permanently narrowed.

3. **Penal Code**
   - **Section 295**: Defiling a place of worship — up to 2 years' jail, fine, or both.
   - **Section 298**: Uttering words with deliberate intent to wound religious feelings — up to 1 year's jail, fine, or both.
   - **Section 298A**: Causing disharmony on grounds of religion — 2 to 5 years' jail. Applied to perceived insults against Islam.

4. **Federal Constitution**
   - **Article 3(1)**: Islam is the religion of the Federation; other religions may be practised in peace and harmony.
   - **Article 8**: Equality before the law (basis on which Court of Appeal struck "offensive/annoy").
   - **Article 10**: Freedom of speech, subject to Parliament-imposed restrictions on grounds including public order and morality.
   - **Article 152**: Bahasa Malaysia as the national language; protections for other languages.
   - **Article 153**: Special position of the Malays and natives of Sabah and Sarawak.
   - **Article 181**: Sovereignty, prerogatives, powers and jurisdiction of the Rulers.

5. **National Harmony & Reconciliation Bill (SKRN)** — Cabinet draft. Per Minister Aaron Dagang's April 2026 statements, takes a "mediation over punishment" approach with Singapore (Maintenance of Religious Harmony Act 1990), Australia (Racial Discrimination Act 1975) and Zimbabwe (National Peace and Reconciliation Commission Act 2017) as reference jurisdictions. Has been "in progress" in some form since Najib's 2012 announcement — 14 years and counting.

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **"Firmer action needed" vs "enforcement is already rising"**: Zahid's call is for a strengthened legal framework. SUARAM (April 2025) and CIJ (2025) both document rising use of existing laws. Both can be true if Zahid's view is that the *current* laws have legal vulnerabilities (which *Heidy Quah* arguably proves). We report both and let the reader weigh them.
- **Court of Appeal *vs* Federal Court status of CMA s.233**: As of brief date, the Court of Appeal's August 2025 ruling stands as binding precedent — the words "offensive" and "annoy" are excised. The Federal Court has granted leave (Nov 2025) but has not yet ruled. We will say *"struck down by the Court of Appeal in August 2025 and currently on Federal Court appeal"* — not *"struck down"* without qualification.
- **SUARAM "five-year high" precise number**: SUARAM's published summary describes a five-year high in Sedition Act charges in 2025; the exact integer is not in the indexed summary. We will report the qualitative finding ("five-year high"), not invent a number.
- **National Harmony Bill: how many drafts and what are they called**: The Bar Council framework is three bills (Harmony & Reconciliation; Harmony & Reconciliation Commission; Racial & Religious Hate Crimes). The current cabinet study, per Minister Aaron Dagang's April 2026 statements, references a Bill and a parallel Commission (SKRN). We will call the current cabinet item the "National Harmony & Reconciliation Bill" and note the Bar Council framework separately.
