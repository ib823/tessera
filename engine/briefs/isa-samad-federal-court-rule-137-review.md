# ISSUE 1992 — Isa Samad's Rule 137 Review: When Two Officers of the Court Disagree on What the Apex Court Said

## ISSUE
Three courts heard the same nine corruption charges against former Felda
chairman Tan Sri Mohd Isa Abdul Samad and reached three different
conclusions: convict (High Court, 2021), acquit (Court of Appeal, 2024),
re-convict (Federal Court, 10 Feb 2026). On 13 May 2026 the Federal
Court adjourned Isa's Rule 137 review of that re-conviction after
allowing both sides access to the Court Recording and Transcription
(CRT) of the 5 and 10 February apex-court proceedings. The defence
asserts that Isa's lawyer was denied submissions on the merits and
mitigation before the verdict; the prosecution asserts the bench asked
and the lawyer declined. The substantive question is not whether Isa is
guilty — that has been tested three times. It is whether the apex
court's routine record can authoritatively resolve a dispute between
two officers of the court about what the court said in open session.

## PERIOD
3 February 2021 (High Court conviction) through 13 May 2026 (Federal
Court Rule 137 review adjournment; case management 14 May to fix new
hearing date).

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **August 2015**: Felda Investment Corporation Sdn Bhd (FICSB), a
  subsidiary of FELDA, purchases Merdeka Palace Hotel & Suites in
  Kuching, Sarawak, from Gegasan Abadi Properties Sdn Bhd (GAPSB) for
  **RM160 million**. Isa Samad is FELDA chairman (2011–2017).
- **12 October 2018**: Mohd Isa charged at Sessions Court Kuala Lumpur
  with nine counts of corruptly receiving **RM3.09 million** in
  gratification from Ikhwan Zaidel (then GAPSB director) through
  Muhammad Zahid Md Arip (Isa's former Special Officer) as inducement
  to approve FICSB's purchase of the Merdeka Palace Hotel. Charges
  under the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission Act 2009.
- **3 February 2021**: High Court (Judge Mohd Nazlan Mohd Ghazali —
  the same judge who convicted Najib Razak in the SRC International
  case at first instance) finds Isa guilty on all nine counts;
  sentences him to **6 years' imprisonment** and a fine of **RM15.45
  million** in default of two years' imprisonment. The Star;
  Malay Mail "Former Felda chief Isa Samad convicted of nine
  corruption charges."
- **6 March 2024**: Court of Appeal (three-judge panel led by Justice
  Datuk Vazeer Alam Mydin Meera) unanimously **acquits and discharges**
  Isa on all nine counts. Court of Appeal reasoning: prosecution
  presented two inconsistent narratives — charges and opening referred
  to gratification as a "reward" for helping approve the purchase,
  trial narrative shifted to "inducement" — and the prosecution failed
  to prove a prima facie case on either framing. The Star "Court of
  Appeal acquits Mohd Isa Samad of corruption charges";
  BenarNews.
- **7 March 2024**: Attorney-General files notice of appeal against the
  acquittal. The Malaysian Reserve.
- **5 July 2025**: Prosecution files its memorandum of appeal listing
  **24 grounds** on which it contends the Court of Appeal erred.
  Malay Mail "Prosecution lists 24 grounds why Court of Appeal erred
  in acquitting Isa Samad."
- **5 February 2026**: Federal Court (three-judge panel chaired by
  Justice Datuk Nordin Hassan) hears the prosecution appeal at the
  apex court. This hearing is one of two open-court sessions whose
  recording is now in dispute.
- **10 February 2026**: Federal Court allows the prosecution appeal,
  sets aside the 2024 acquittal, and reinstates the 2021 conviction
  and sentence (6 years jail + RM15.45M fine). Warrant of committal
  issued. Findings include that the elements of the offence were
  proven beyond a reasonable doubt for all nine counts; that Isa
  failed to rebut the **statutory presumption under MACC Act Section
  50(1)**; and that the trial-court finding of solicitation was
  preserved (evidence cited Isa instructing Zahid in colloquial Malay
  to "ambil lah" any payment offered). NST "Why the Federal Court
  reinstated Isa Samad's corruption conviction"; Bernama "Federal
  Court Reinstates Isa Samad's Conviction And Sentence, Issues Warrant
  Of Committal."
- **4 March 2026**: Isa files for review under **Rule 137 of the
  Federal Court Rules 1995**, contending that his counsel M Athimulan
  was not given the right of reply / opportunity to make submissions
  on his behalf before the 10 February verdict. The Star "Isa Samad
  files review against Federal Court's decision restoring corruption
  conviction, fine and jail term"; FMT "Isa files review to set aside
  corruption conviction."
- **22 April 2026**: Apex court fixes 13 May for the Rule 137 review
  hearing. The Star; Bernama; Malay Mail "May 13 hearing for Isa
  Samad's bid to review prison term, RM15.45m fine."
- **13 May 2026**: Federal Court three-judge panel chaired by Chief
  Judge of Malaya Datuk Seri Hashim Hamzah (with Chief Judge of Sabah
  and Sarawak Justice Azizah Nawawi, and Federal Court judge Justice
  Collin Lawrence Sequerah) **adjourns** the review hearing. The
  panel allows the defence's request for both parties to be supplied
  with the Court Recording and Transcription (CRT) of the 5 February
  and 10 February proceedings "in the interest of justice." The
  request was made because of conflicting accounts of what occurred
  in open court at the original Federal Court hearing. The Star
  "Federal Court defers Isa Samad review bid, allows access to court
  transcripts"; FMT "Federal Court adjourns Isa Samad's bid to
  overturn conviction"; Malay Mail "Federal Court adjourns Isa
  Samad's bid to review reinstated corruption conviction."
- **14 May 2026**: Case management scheduled to fix new hearing date
  for the substantive review application. New date not in public
  reporting at brief drafting (17 May 2026).

## THE DISPUTE OVER WHAT TRANSPIRED

This is the substantive issue the CRT is meant to resolve:

- **Defence counsel M Athimulan (also reported as Mohamed Athimulan /
  MM Athimulan):** The 10 February panel did not invite the defence
  to submit oral arguments on the merits of the defence and
  mitigation after Isa was found liable. The CRT for 5 February and
  10 February is needed to ascertain whether the bench so invited.
- **Deputy public prosecutor Afzainizam Abdul Aziz:** The three-member
  Federal Court bench did ask Athimulan if he wished to make oral
  submissions on Isa's defence and mitigation, and Athimulan replied
  in the negative.

Both positions are now on the public record, asserted in open court
on 13 May. The CRT is the proposed neutral arbiter. The fact that the
written record of the 10 February proceedings is not by itself treated
as conclusive — that the audio recording must be supplied to both
sides — is itself a structural finding.

## RELEVANT LAW

- **Rule 137, Rules of the Federal Court 1995 (P.U.(A) 376/1995)** —
  preserves the inherent power of the Federal Court to "make any order
  as may be necessary to prevent injustice or to prevent an abuse of
  the process of the Court." The jurisdiction is exercised in **rare
  and exceptional circumstances** only: lack of quorum, established
  bias, or where it is shown the integrity of the earlier decision
  was critically undermined. The power cannot be invoked to relitigate
  merits. (Reference: Cecil Abraham & Partners; Joshua Wu Kai-Ming;
  Neoh Kai Sheng practitioner notes — see SOURCES.)
- **MACC Act 2009, Section 16 / Section 17(a)** — corrupt acceptance
  of gratification by an agent (the charges Isa faced).
- **MACC Act 2009, Section 50(1)** — statutory **presumption of
  corruption**: where it is proved that gratification has been accepted
  by an agent, it is presumed to have been corruptly received unless
  the contrary is proved. This was the presumption Isa "failed to
  rebut" per the 10 February 2026 Federal Court reasoning.
- **Federal Constitution Article 5(1)** — no person shall be deprived
  of life or personal liberty save in accordance with law. Established
  to include the rule of natural justice (audi alteram partem — hear
  the other side) at the apex level.
- **Federal Constitution Article 8(1)** — equality before the law.
- **Federal Constitution Article 145** — Attorney-General's
  prosecutorial discretion; basis for the 2024 prosecution appeal.
- **Courts of Judicature Act 1964, Sections 87 and 92** — the
  appellate framework under which the prosecution appealed the
  Court of Appeal's acquittal to the Federal Court.

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **What was said at the 10 February hearing about defence
  submissions** — This is the substantive disputed fact and the
  reason the CRT was ordered. The two positions are:
  - Defence (M Athimulan, asserted 13 May): the bench did not invite
    submissions on the merits and mitigation.
  - Prosecution (DPP Afzainizam Abdul Aziz, asserted 13 May): the
    bench did invite, and the defence declined.
  T4A position: report both, in their own words on the record, and
  do not adjudicate. The CRT is the neutral instrument the court has
  ordered released for that purpose. Any card framing must preserve
  both positions until the CRT outcome is in the public record.

- **Filing date of the Rule 137 review** — The Star (7 March 2026)
  and FMT (7 March 2026) report the filing as having occurred on
  4 March 2026 ("On March 4, Isa filed for a review under Rule 137").
  Use **4 March 2026** as the filing date, **7 March 2026** as the
  reporting date.

- **Bribery amount: RM3 million vs RM3.09 million vs RM3.1 million**
  — Different outlets round differently. The High Court particulars
  cite **RM3.09 million** as the aggregate across nine counts.
  Cards should use **RM3.09 million** with the option of "more than
  RM3 million" in the headline if character count is tight. Do not
  use RM3.1M unless directly quoting a source that uses it.

- **Conviction date 2021 vs "2018"** — Some Federal Court / Bernama
  summary lines say the apex court "restored the High Court's **2018**
  conviction," conflating the **charging** date (12 Oct 2018) with
  the **conviction** date (3 Feb 2021). The High Court conviction is
  3 Feb 2021. Cards must use 2021.

## EDITORIAL FRAME (provisional, for Stage 1)

The mainstream framings sort along two well-worn tracks:
- "Disgraced UMNO veteran tries one last appeal" (procedural-as-
  inevitable, conviction-as-final).
- "Defence wins access to recordings — fresh window for review"
  (procedural-as-opening, conviction-as-fragile).

Both frames assume the substantive question is Isa's guilt. It is not.
That has been ruled on three times and is now on appeal a fourth time.
The T4A distinct contribution is **the structural question about the
apex-court record itself**:

1. **What does it mean that two officers of the court disagree on
   record about what the apex court said in open session?**
   The DPP and the defence counsel are bound by the same professional
   obligations to the court. When they assert irreconcilable accounts
   of the same hearing, the routine paper record was not enough — both
   sides had to apply for, and the bench had to release, the audio
   recording.

2. **What is the right of reply at the Federal Court when the
   prosecution appeals an acquittal?** If the defence is found not to
   have been invited to submit on merits and mitigation, the
   conviction reinstated on 10 February was reached without one side
   fully heard at the apex level — and that is precisely the lack of
   natural justice (audi alteram partem) that Rule 137 review exists
   to remedy.

3. **Why is the CRT not the default record?** A judiciary that treats
   the paper minute as authoritative until contested, then has to
   release the audio recording to resolve contests, has effectively
   admitted that the routine record is not by itself reliable for
   high-stakes proceedings. The structural reform question is whether
   the CRT should simply be the record from the start.

The reframe should rotate readers from "will Isa walk?" to **"When
the apex court's record is contested, what counts as the record?"**

The view card should pass the WhatsApp test: a sentence a thoughtful
reader would paste into a group chat with no commentary. A working
draft: *"Three courts heard the same nine charges and reached three
different conclusions. The fourth round is not about whether Isa is
guilty. It is about whether the third round was heard."*
