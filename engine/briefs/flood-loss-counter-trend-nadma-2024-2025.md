# ISSUE 1998 — Malaysia's Flood Bill Fell By A Third In 2025. The Number Says Less Than Headlines Suggest.

## ISSUE
On 15 April 2026, the Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM)
released its *Special Report on Impact of Floods in Malaysia 2025*.
The headline finding: total flood-related losses in Malaysia fell from
**RM933.4 million in 2024 to RM636.9 million in 2025** — a **31.8%
year-on-year decline**, equivalent to **0.03% of nominal GDP**. In a
news cycle where the dominant frame for Malaysian floods is
"monotonically worsening climate damage," the official number points
the other way. The substantive question is *what the number is and is
not*. DOSM's figure is a **direct, surveyed property-and-infrastructure
damage estimate**: living quarters, vehicles, business premises,
agricultural land, manufacturing, and public assets/infrastructure.
It does not include indirect economic loss (business interruption,
productivity drag), human cost (deaths, displacement), or events that
fell outside the calendar-year sampling frame. Three explanations
compete for the 32% decline: (1) **mitigation effect** — drainage,
retention-basin and post-flood remediation work between 2024 and 2025
worked at the margin; (2) **weather variance** — 2024 was an unusually
severe year (the December 2024 east coast event displaced 137,000+
people and was the worst since 2014), while 2025's main monsoon-end
events (Cyclone Senyar, 26 November 2025, plus the December 2025
nationwide floods) caused fewer dwelling-level losses than 2024
despite killing three and displacing 34,000+; (3) **structural shift**
— public-asset/infrastructure damage actually **rose 25%** to
RM380.2M, while household-level damage fell sharply. The reframe is
that the *type* of damage is shifting from private property to public
infrastructure even as the headline number falls, which is consistent
with the World Bank's repeated finding that the true economic cost of
flooding sits well above what DOSM's direct-damage methodology
captures. T4A's distinct contribution is to refuse the easy "good
news vs climate doom" frame and walk readers through what the number
counts.

## ACTORS

- **Department of Statistics Malaysia (DOSM)** — federal statistics
  agency; primary author of the *Special Report on Impact of Floods*
  series; uses a special-survey methodology to estimate direct damage
  on a calendar-year basis. Reports to the Ministry of Economy.
- **Datuk Seri Dr Mohd Uzir Mahidin** — Chief Statistician of
  Malaysia; signatory of the 15 April 2026 release; the named
  spokesperson for the RM636.9M figure.
- **National Disaster Management Agency (NADMA)** — coordinating
  agency under the Prime Minister's Department; runs evacuation
  centres, BWI aid disbursement, and the disaster operations control
  centres (PKOBs). Headed since 2026 by **Meor Ismail Meor Akim**;
  previously **Datuk Khairul Shahril Idrus**.
- **Datuk Seri Dr Ahmad Zahid Hamidi** — Deputy Prime Minister and
  chairman of the **National Disaster Management Committee** (the
  cabinet-level oversight body); public face of the government on
  monsoon preparedness and on the "global model" claim.
- **Datuk Seri Fadillah Yusof** — Deputy Prime Minister (Energy
  Transition and Water Transformation portfolio); also the federal
  voice on Sarawak-specific flood mitigation (the RM2.6B Kuching plan).
- **Datuk Seri Nik Nazmi Nik Ahmad** — Minister of Natural Resources
  and Environmental Sustainability (NRES) **until 4 July 2025** (when
  he resigned after losing his PKR party post); custodian of the
  pending Climate Change Bill which lists "how to deal with natural
  disasters" as a focus area.
- **Tuan Nga Kor Ming** — Minister of Housing and Local Government
  (KPKT); jointly responsible with NRES for nature-based flood
  mitigation; allocated RM219.6M (2023–2024) to drainage and
  retention-pond upgrades, RM108.5M to Sungai Kinta works.
- **Department of Irrigation and Drainage (JPS/DID)** — under the
  Ministry of Energy Transition and Water Transformation; operational
  authority for river systems, hydrological forecasting, retention
  basins, and the SMART Tunnel system in KL. Has identified **4,619
  flood hotspots nationwide**.
- **Malaysian Meteorological Department (MetMalaysia)** — issues
  monsoon forecasts; identified the **weak La Niña** conditioning the
  2025/2026 season.
- **Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) + World Bank** — joint authors of the
  2024 report estimating floods could cost up to **4.1% of economic
  output by 2030** under climate-change scenarios; lead voices arguing
  DOSM's direct-damage methodology understates the true economic toll.
- **Persatuan Insurans Am Malaysia (PIAM)** — general insurance
  association; data shows **74% of Malaysian homes are uninsured
  against flood damage** (consistent with the DOSM 2025 finding that
  only 5.5% of victims had insurance).
- **Malaysian Red Crescent Society / MERCY Malaysia / IFRC** —
  community-facing flood-response organisations; the IFRC DREF
  Operation MDRMY011 covered the December 2024 east coast emergency.
- **State governments** — Kelantan, Terengganu, Pahang, Johor,
  Sarawak, Sabah, Selangor — operational front line; receive federal
  mitigation funds. Sarawak's RM2.6B Kuching plan is the largest
  pending state-level project as of April 2026.
- **World Bank Malaysia Country Office** — produces the *Managing
  Flood Risks: Leveraging Finance for Business Resilience in Malaysia*
  series and the Country Climate and Development Report, which set
  the long-horizon GDP-at-risk numbers (up to 16% by 2050).

## RELEVANT LAW

- **NADMA Directive No. 1** — the principal operational framework for
  disaster management in Malaysia (rather than a dedicated statute).
  Defines roles of federal, state and district disaster operations
  control centres. NADMA was established **1 October 2015** by merger
  of the National Security Council Disaster Management Division, the
  Prime Minister's Department Post-Flood Recovery Unit, and the
  SMART rescue team. No standalone "National Disaster Management Act"
  yet exists.
- **National Disaster Risk Reduction Policy 2030** — issued by NADMA;
  the medium-term strategy document targeted at FATF/UN Sendai
  Framework alignment.
- **National Security Council Directive No. 20** — predecessor
  directive on the management of disasters; still operative for the
  interagency coordination architecture.
- **Town and Country Planning Act 1976 (Act 172)** — provides for
  flood-zone land-use controls at local-authority level; long
  identified by NADMA, JPS and the World Bank as **underenforced** in
  flood-prone development decisions.
- **Local Government Act 1976 (Act 171)** — empowers local authorities
  to maintain drainage and retention systems; the operating statute
  behind the KPKT RM219.6M drainage upgrade programme.
- **Waters Act 1920** and **Drainage Works Ordinance 1954** (peninsular)
  — historical statutes on the regulation of rivers and drainage,
  relevant to JPS authority.
- **Climate Change Bill (pending)** — listed by NRES as targeted for
  enactment in **2026**, with explicit treatment of natural-disaster
  response. Not in force at brief drafting.
- **Financial Services Act 2013** (BNM remit) — backstop for the
  general-insurance market; relevant to the question of why flood
  insurance penetration sits at ~5.5% of victims and 26% of homes.
- **Capital Markets and Services Act 2007** — relevant to disaster
  bonds and resilience-finance instruments (a World Bank-flagged
  policy lever still largely unused by Malaysia in this domain).

**Long-horizon climate cost (World Bank + BNM):**
- **Up to 4.1%** of economic output that floods could cost by 2030
  under World Bank/BNM 2024 modelling.
- **Up to 16%** of GDP that climate impacts could reduce by 2050
  under the Country Climate and Development Report worst case
  (annual losses could pass 20% in a single extreme year).
- **~3%** average reduction in firm revenues and productivity over
  2015–2024 attributed to heavy rainfall, equivalent to ~US$7
  billion in cumulative GDP losses (World Bank).
- **85%** of all natural disasters in Malaysia since 2000 are floods.
- **74%** of Malaysian homes are uninsured against flood damage.

**Methodology gap to label:**
- DOSM's number is **direct, surveyed property and infrastructure
  damage** for the calendar year — it excludes business-interruption
  losses, lost productivity, displaced wages, and the human cost
  (deaths and prolonged displacement). The World Bank/BNM 4.1%-of-GDP
  figure is an order of magnitude larger because it counts what DOSM's
  methodology does not.

## 12-DIMENSION RISK ASSESSMENT

1. **Sentiment** — MEDIUM: There is a risk readers hear "32% drop" as
   "the climate problem is over." Cards must hold a careful register:
   the number is real, the underlying climate-exposure trajectory is
   not improving in the same direction.
2. **Political** — MEDIUM: Government voices (DPM Zahid, DPM Fadillah,
   Minister Nga, DOSM under the Ministry of Economy) will frame the
   2025 fall as policy success. Opposition voices will frame the
   methodology gap as the real story. T4A's frame is structural and
   non-partisan: what does the number count, and what does it not.
3. **Ethnic** — LOW: Floods affect all ethnic communities; the
   geographic concentration (east coast, Borneo) maps to majority
   Malay and Bumiputera populations but the issue is not ethnic.
4. **Religious** — LOW: Not implicated.
5. **Narrative** — HIGH: This is precisely a narrative-collision
   issue. "Climate change is monotonically worsening" vs "official
   data shows decline." Both framings miss the structural point that
   direct-damage methodology systematically underestimates climate
   cost. T4A's contribution lives in that gap.
6. **Completeness** — HIGH: DOSM's methodology is narrow (direct
   property and infrastructure damage only); the World Bank-and-BNM
   models capture far more (business interruption, productivity).
   Cards must label what the number includes and excludes.
7. **Temporal** — MEDIUM: The DOSM 2025 report covers Jan–Dec 2025
   inclusive of Cyclone Senyar (26 Nov 2025) and the December 2025
   peak displacement. Cards must not imply the 2025 reference period
   excluded the late-November/December events — it did not.
8. **Confidence** — HIGH on DOSM figures (an official statistical
   agency release with named spokesperson); HIGH on the historical
   baseline (multiple DOSM reports); MEDIUM on the *causal*
   attribution of the 2025 decline (mitigation vs weather variance vs
   methodological consistency).
9. **Sources** — HIGH: Bernama, The Star, Edge Malaysia, FMT, Malay
   Mail, Eco-Business, DOSM directly, BNM/World Bank reports, NADMA,
   MetMalaysia, IFRC, ReliefWeb. Spectrum: government-aligned,
   centrist independent, international body, environmental press.
10. **Geographic** — HIGH: Story is national. East coast (Kelantan,
    Terengganu, Pahang) is the historical flood corridor; Borneo
    (Sarawak, Sabah) is the rising-risk corridor (Sarawak living
    quarters losses 14× higher in 2025 vs 2024); Klang Valley is the
    catastrophic-risk corridor (the 2021 baseline).
11. **Economic** — HIGH: The headline is fundamentally an economic
    number, and the methodology question is the editorial heart of
    this issue. Cards must be precise on what RM636.9M is and is not.
12. **Gender** — LOW: Not a gendered story at the first order.

**3R note**: LOW across Race/Religion/Royalty. The Sarawak Premier
and DPM Fadillah are political figures, not royalty. No religious
or ethnic framing.

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **"2024 flood loss = RM933.4M, a 23% increase" vs "RM933.4M, a 24%
  increase"**: FMT framed the 2023→2024 jump as 24%; The Star framed
  it as a "surge"; the exact arithmetic from RM755.4M → RM933.4M is
  **23.6%**. Cards should use "rose ~24% in 2024" or "the highest
  figure in three years."

- **2025 deaths — Cyclone Senyar Malaysia toll**: Different sources
  give **2** and **3** Malaysian deaths from the late-November 2025
  Senyar event. Britannica/Watchers report 3 (including 2 in
  Kelantan); Al Jazeera as of 9 December reports 2. **Use "at least
  3" with attribution** in cards if deaths come up; the DOSM RM figure
  does **not** include deaths so this is a tangential not central
  number.

- **DOSM publication date pattern**: The 2024 report was released
  19 March 2025 (URL `file_20250319122430.pdf` confirms). The 2025
  report was released **15 April 2026** — about four weeks later in
  the annual cycle than 2024. Likely explained by the heavier
  reference-period sampling load (Senyar, December displacement)
  rather than methodology change. The April 2026 release date is what
  every reputable Malaysian outlet (Bernama, Malay Mail, The Edge,
  Eco-Business) reports, and is the correct figure.

- **State-level state ranking in 2025**: Eco-Business reports
  **Terengganu RM89.2M, Kelantan RM88.5M, Johor RM72.1M, Sarawak
  RM61.2M, Sabah RM58.4M, Selangor RM52.9M**. No conflict observed
  across outlets — use these.

- **Public-asset damage rose but total fell**: The fact that
  RM380.2M (2025 public assets) exceeded RM303.4M (2024 public
  assets) **despite** the overall total falling RM296.5M is *not* a
  contradiction; it is the central editorial point. The mix shifted.

- **Total flood-loss ratio to GDP**: DOSM reports the 2025 figure
  as **0.03% of nominal GDP**. The World Bank's accompanying analysis
  reports the 2024 figure as **0.05% of GDP**. The arithmetic checks
  (RM933.4M / RM2.0T ≈ 0.047%; RM636.9M / RM2.1T ≈ 0.030%) — these
  are different years on a slightly different GDP base, not a
  contradiction.

- **NADMA director-general 2025/2026**: Datuk Khairul Shahril Idrus
  was DG in earlier 2025 statements; **Meor Ismail Meor Akim** is DG
  by January 2026 (the BWI processing statement). Cards should
  attribute the BWI/aid number to Meor; if quoting earlier 2025
  monsoon-preparation statements, attribute to Khairul Shahril.

- **NRES minister timing**: **Nik Nazmi resigned 4 July 2025**.
  Statements about the Climate Change Bill prior to that date are
  attributable to him; later statements should not be. (Successor
  attribution check is a Stage 3 task.)

## EDITORIAL FRAME (provisional, for Stage 1)

Mainstream framings of this story fall into three predictable bins:

1. **Triumphalist** ("Floods fell 32% — climate adaptation working"):
   the implicit story line in DPM Zahid's "global model" speech and
   in the Bernama framing. True at the level of the headline number;
   misses that the number counts a narrow slice of climate cost and
   that the **public-infrastructure share rose 25%**.
2. **Climate-doom** ("Don't be fooled, climate change is still
   getting worse"): the framing implicit in the World Bank's parallel
   warning. True at the level of long-horizon GDP risk; misses the
   short-run fact that 2025 *was* less damaging on the dimensions
   DOSM measures, and that this is not nothing.
3. **Methodology-critique** ("The number is meaningless because it
   excludes business interruption"): true; misses that the number is
   still the cleanest year-on-year series Malaysia produces on direct
   flood damage and is internally consistent year over year.

T4A's distinct contribution is **the structural mix-shift**: even as
the headline figure fell 32%, the share borne by **public assets and
infrastructure rose to 60% of the total** (RM380.2M / RM636.9M), up
from 33% in 2024 (RM303.4M / RM933.4M). The damage that *did* happen
in 2025 fell more on the things the public sector pays to rebuild
(roads, bridges, drainage, schools) and less on the things households
and businesses pay to rebuild themselves. That is a meaningful
finding hidden inside the "32% fall" framing.

The reframe should rotate readers from **"did floods get cheaper?"**
to **"who paid for the floods that did happen?"** The view card
should pass the WhatsApp test and resist both the triumphalist and
the doom framings.

Working WhatsApp-test view-card sentence (draft):
*"Floods got cheaper for households in 2025. They got more expensive
for the public works budget. Both can be true."*

Alternative: *"The bill fell by RM296 million. The share borne by
the public infrastructure budget rose by RM77 million. The story is
in the mix, not the total."*

## STAGE 1 GUIDANCE

The reader needs to leave with three things:

1. The official DOSM number is real and is the cleanest year-on-year
   series Malaysia produces — a **32% drop in headline flood damage,
   to RM636.9 million in 2025**.
2. The **same report** shows public-asset and infrastructure damage
   went **up 25%** to RM380.2M, while household-level (living quarters)
   damage fell 50% to RM183.8M. The composition of damage shifted
   toward what the public works budget pays for.
3. The DOSM figure is a **direct, surveyed damage** number — it does
   not include business interruption, lost productivity, deaths, or
   prolonged displacement. The World Bank's separate modelling
   (climate impacts could cost up to 4.1% of GDP by 2030) sits in a
   different methodology and is the right counterweight to the
   "32% drop = problem solved" framing.

The reframe must restructure the question from "did floods get
cheaper?" to "who paid for the floods that happened?" The view card
must pass the WhatsApp test — a single sentence a thoughtful reader
would paste into a group chat with no commentary.

Recommended fact-card lens distribution:
- Fact 1 (Environmental lens) — the headline DOSM number with the
  reference period and the named release date.
- Fact 2 (Economic lens) — the mix shift: public-asset share rose,
  household share fell. This is the issue's most-shocking-number
  card per the Berger-Milkman pacing rule.
- Fact 3 (Governance lens) — the methodology gap: what DOSM counts
  and does not. The World Bank's wider damage estimate sits here.

The analogy card is **optional** — flood damage as house insurance
where the policy excludes business interruption is one workable
shape, but only if it carries its weight; otherwise drop it.
