# ISSUE 1981 — Two MA63 delays: Sabah pauses AKPS while courts pause Sabah's 40%

## ISSUE
Two parallel "delays" landed five weeks apart, both anchored on the Malaysia Agreement 1963 (MA63):

1. **6 April 2026 — Putrajaya pauses Sabah's 40%.** The Court of Appeal granted the Federal Government a stay of execution on the Kota Kinabalu High Court order (17 October 2025, Judge Datuk Celestina Stuel Galid) requiring a mandatory review of Sabah's 40% net-federal-revenue entitlement under Article 112C/112D of the Federal Constitution, covering the 1974-2021 "lost years." Court-of-Appeal panel: Mohamed Zaini Mazlan, Ismail Brahim, K. Muniandy. The Attorney General's Chambers (AGC) was careful to state that the **stay does not affect Sabah's 40% entitlement or formula itself** — it pauses the High Court's payment-calculation directives and findings of mutual constitutional breach pending appeal.
2. **13 May 2026 — Sabah pauses Putrajaya's AKPS.** The Sabah Cabinet, chaired by Chief Minister Datuk Seri Hajiji Noor, deferred the rollout of the Malaysian Border Control and Protection Agency (AKPS), citing inconsistencies between the AKPS Act 2024 (Act 860) and the Immigration Act 1959/63 (Act 155) and the risk that the AKPS structure would leave Sabah with only "indirect oversight" of border operations within its own jurisdiction. The Sabah Law Society (SLS), the Sabah Immigration Officers Service Union (KPPIS), and former CM Tan Sri Salleh Said Keruak all publicly backed the deferral within 48 hours.

The headline mainstream picture treats these as two separate stories. The picture the radar accumulated data supports is one bidirectional MA63 dynamic: each side uses procedurally legitimate tools to pause the other, but the framing of those tools differs sharply by political vantage. The SLS itself described the federal stay as "procedural"; federal voices have not consistently returned that framing about Sabah's deferral. That asymmetry — same agreement, different label — is the editorial angle.

## PERIOD
17 October 2025 (KK High Court 40% ruling) → 6 April 2026 (Court of Appeal stay) → 9 May 2026 (Bernama / DayakDaily — Sabah Cabinet preview of AKPS concerns) → 13 May 2026 (Sabah deferral announcement) → 14-15 May 2026 (SLS, KPPIS, Salleh, SabahSTAR, UMNO Putatan backings).

## CONTEXT (Timeline)

- **1963 — Malaysia Agreement (MA63)**: Sabah and Sarawak join the Federation. Annex A's State Constitution and the Federal Constitution carry the **special grant** (Article 112C, Tenth Schedule Part IV) and the **quinquennial review** requirement (Article 112D). Immigration into Sabah and Sarawak is reserved to the state under Article IX of the Immigration Act 1959/63.
- **1969**: First and only Article 112D review takes place, covering 1963-1973; agrees on a special grant of RM26.7 million per year.
- **1974**: The five-year review window opens. No review takes place. The annual special grant remains pegged at the 1969 figure.
- **1974-2021 — the "lost years"**: For 48 successive years, no Article 112D review is held. Sabah continues to receive RM26.7 million annually as the special grant — the same nominal figure agreed in 1969. (Source: Borneo Post "Sabah's Lost Years" explainer, 12 Nov 2025.)
- **3 October 2024**: Federal Court grants Sabah Law Society leave to bring the judicial review challenging Putrajaya's failure to conduct the Article 112D reviews. (Source: The Vibes; NST.)
- **17 October 2025 — Kota Kinabalu High Court judgment**: Judge Datuk Celestina Stuel Galid delivers a 107-page judgment in *Sabah Law Society v Government of Malaysia & Ors*. Findings: both the Federal Government **and** the Sabah Government breached Article 112D(1), (3) and (4) by failing to conduct the mandated reviews from 1974 to 2021. Putrajaya's issuance of a "special grant" from 2022 to 2025 — derived from the 1969 figure — is declared **unlawful**. Orders: (a) the two governments must conduct the mandatory review covering 1974-2021 within **90 days**; (b) reach agreement within **180 days** of the order. Standard of relief: Order of Mandamus. (Sources: FMT, The Star, Borneo Post, theedgemalaysia.com IN THE COURTS 2025.)
- **14 January 2026**: Sabah state assembly representatives publicly question what they call "lack of good-faith compliance" with the 17 October 2025 court order. SLS warns that delay in the Article 112D review "will undermine the court ruling." (Source: The Star, 14 Jan 2026.)
- **31 March 2026**: Court of Appeal in Putrajaya concludes the hearing of Putrajaya's stay application. Decision reserved to Monday 6 April 2026. (Sources: The Star, FMT, DayakDaily, NST.)
- **6 April 2026 — Court of Appeal grants stay**: Three-member panel of Justices Mohamed Zaini Mazlan, Ismail Brahim and K. Muniandy unanimously grants the Federal Government's application for an interim stay of execution pending disposal of the appeal. Justice Mohamed Zaini's reasoning quoted in multiple outlets: "The payments ordered are of such magnitude that their execution, prior to the determination of the appeal, would create a fait accompli." The bench also notes that the Federal Government **accepts** Sabah's 40% entitlement but disputes how the High Court computed it. (Sources: Bernama, The Star, FMT, Malay Mail, The Vibes, The Edge.)
- **6 April 2026 — AGC clarification**: The Attorney General's Chambers issues a statement: the federal appeal is **not against the 40% formula itself** and does **not affect Sabah's 40% entitlement**. The appeal targets the High Court's findings on constitutional interpretation and on mutual breach of constitutional duties since 1974. (Sources: Malay Mail; The Vibes; The Edge Malaysia node 798778.)
- **6 April 2026 — SLS response**: SLS describes the Court of Appeal stay as "procedural" — the High Court's ruling that the federal government failed to honour Article 112D for 48 years still stands legally; only the operational orders (review timetable and payment computation) are paused. (Sources: Malay Mail "SLS says Putrajaya stay 'procedural'"; The Star "High Court ruling on 40% rights still stands despite appeals court's stay order, says SLS".)
- **6 April 2026 — political response**: Sabah Deputy Chief Minister Datuk Seri Dr Jeffrey Kitingan and state-level coalition partners publicly reaffirm that the underlying 40% right is not in dispute, only the timing. Mustapha Sakmud (Sabah PKR) says the stay will not stop work on Sabah's 40% entitlement. (Sources: The Star "Mustapha"; DayakDaily.)
- **April 2026 — federal-state negotiation track**: Sabah Finance Minister Datuk Seri Masidi Manjun and the state Finance Office continue technical engagement with Putrajaya on the Article 112D review parameters. No new bilateral agreement is signed.
- **10 May 2026 — KPPIS raises Act 860 concerns**: Sabah Immigration Officers Service Union (KPPIS, president Zaki Susanto) raises formal concerns to the Sabah State Cabinet about the Malaysian Border Control and Protection Agency Act 2024 (Act 860), particularly its interaction with Sabah's special immigration powers under the Immigration Act 1959/63. (Sources: Borneo Post 10 May; Daily Express 10 May.)
- **11 May 2026 — Jesselton Times commentary**: "Do not touch Sabah's immigration autonomy — MA63 is not a symbol, it is a constitutional guarantee." (Editorial line being argued in state.)
- **12 May 2026 — KDM response**: Parti Kesejahteraan Demokratik Masyarakat (KDM, a Sabah state-coalition partner) backs KPPIS's concerns, calling them "sovereign rights" issues. (Borneo Post, 12 May.)
- **13 May 2026 — Warisan response**: Warisan reps echo the same concern publicly, warning Act 860 could "erode Sabah's special immigration powers" guaranteed under MA63. (Borneo Post, 13 May.)
- **13 May 2026 — Sabah Cabinet decision**: Chaired by CM Hajiji Noor, the Sabah State Cabinet decides to defer the AKPS policy and implementation in Sabah pending: (i) a joint legal audit reconciling Act 860 with the Immigration Act 1959/63, and (ii) assurances that Sabah's immigration autonomy under MA63 will not be eroded. Hajiji's stated position: "Sabah's immigration autonomy, together with Sarawak's, is non-negotiable as it forms part of the safeguards enshrined under the Malaysia Agreement 1963." Specific concern flagged: under the AKPS Act's Section 6(3), Sabah would exercise only **indirect oversight** of the federal agency operating in the state, through the Sabah immigration director — not direct executive control. (Sources: The Star 13 May; Borneo Post 13 May; FMT 13 May; Daily Express 13 May; Jesselton Times 13 May; Bernama via Borneo Post.)
- **13 May 2026 — Salleh Said Keruak public statement**: Former Sabah CM Tan Sri Salleh Said Keruak calls the deferral "prudent and justified" and an "important reaffirmation of Sabah's constitutional rights." He says ambiguity in AKPS implementation risks "weakening long-established state powers in immigration matters." (Sources: FMT 13 May; Jesselton Times 14 May.)
- **14 May 2026 — SLS public statement**: Sabah Law Society president Datuk Mohamed Nazim says the State Cabinet's deferral demonstrates "legal foresight" by prioritising constitutional certainty over administrative expediency. SLS offers to assist with a comprehensive legal audit. (Sources: Borneo Post 14 May; The Star 14 May "Preserve Sabah's rights, get border laws in sync before enforcing AKPS, says lawyers group".)
- **14 May 2026 — KPPIS public statement**: Sabah Immigration Officers Service Union welcomes the deferral; thanks Hajiji's administration for swift executive action protecting MA63 rights. (Borneo Post.)
- **14 May 2026 — SabahSTAR president**: Public statement saying Sabah "should not compromise on immigration powers under MA63 amid AKPS issue." (Borneo Post.)
- **15 May 2026 — UMNO Putatan division**: Public statement reaffirming support for MA63 and backing Sabah's immigration autonomy under the deferred AKPS rollout. Significant because UMNO is a federal coalition partner. (Borneo Post.)

## RELEVANT LAW

1. **Federal Constitution**
   - **Article 112C**: Special grants to Sabah and Sarawak — sets out the constitutional entitlement that the Tenth Schedule Part IV operationalises (including the 40% net-revenue formula).
   - **Article 112D**: Five-year review mechanism for the special grants. The provision the High Court found Putrajaya in breach of for the 1974-2021 period.
   - **Tenth Schedule, Part IV**: Operational text of the 40% net-federal-revenue special grant to Sabah; specifies what counts as "net revenue" and the review obligation.
   - **Article IX of the Immigration Act 1959/63** (read with the Immigration Act): the source of Sabah and Sarawak's reserved immigration powers — the autonomy the AKPS deferral seeks to protect.

2. **Statutes**
   - **Immigration Act 1959/63 (Act 155)** — operative immigration law. Sections governing entry into Sabah/Sarawak reserve special powers to the state director-general.
   - **Malaysian Border Control and Protection Agency Act 2024 (Act 860)** — establishes AKPS as a federal enforcement agency consolidating immigration, customs and security functions at land/sea/air entry points. **Section 6(3)** is the contested provision — the section under which Sabah's role in AKPS operations would be "indirect oversight" through the Sabah immigration director rather than direct executive control.

3. **Court rulings (in chronological order)**
   - **3 October 2024**: Federal Court grants leave to SLS to bring the judicial review.
   - **17 October 2025**: Kota Kinabalu High Court (Judge Celestina Stuel Galid) finds Putrajaya in breach of Article 112D; orders 90-day review and 180-day agreement timeline; declares the 2022-2025 special grants unlawful.
   - **6 April 2026**: Court of Appeal (Mohamed Zaini Mazlan, Ismail Brahim, K. Muniandy) grants interim stay of execution pending appeal; preserves the High Court's findings on legal principle but pauses operational orders. **SLS interpretation: "procedural" stay only; High Court ruling on 40% rights still stands.**

## KEY STATISTICS

- **40%** — Sabah's net-federal-revenue entitlement under Article 112C / Tenth Schedule Part IV. (Source: Federal Constitution, AGC consolidated text.)
- **RM26.7 million** — annual fixed special grant Sabah received from 1969 through 2021. The figure agreed at the first and only Article 112D review in 1969. (Source: Borneo Post "Lost Years" explainer, 12 Nov 2025; High Court judgment.)
- **48 years** — the "lost years": 1974 to 2021 inclusive, the span over which no Article 112D review took place. (Source: High Court judgment 17 Oct 2025; corroborated The Star, FMT, Borneo Post.)
- **107 pages** — length of Judge Celestina Stuel Galid's written judgment. (Source: Jesselton Times "Aftermath" explainer, 20 Oct 2025.)
- **90 days / 180 days** — the High Court's ordered timeline for (a) the mandatory Article 112D review and (b) a federal-state agreement. (High Court judgment; The Star "Judge: Federal Govt must pay Sabah 40% revenue entitlement" 18 Oct 2025.)
- **6 April 2026** — Court of Appeal stay date. (Bernama; multi-outlet.)
- **13 May 2026** — Sabah Cabinet's AKPS deferral decision date.
- **5 weeks** — gap between the two delays. (6 April → 13 May = 37 days; "five weeks" is editorial rounding of an arithmetic fact.)
- **48 hours** — interval over which SLS, KPPIS, Salleh Said Keruak, KDM, Warisan, SabahSTAR, and UMNO Putatan all publicly backed the AKPS deferral after the 13 May Cabinet decision. (Inferred from publication dates 13-15 May 2026, multiple outlets.)
- **Section 6(3) of Act 860** — the AKPS Act provision that places Sabah's oversight of the federal border agency in "indirect" form via the Sabah immigration director. (Source: AKPS Act 2024 statutory text; CM Hajiji's 13 May statement.)

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **Stay's effect on the 40% right**: One reading (Malay Mail headline tone, federal-government appeal track) is that the stay places the entire 40% mechanism in suspense. Another reading (AGC clarification, SLS "procedural" framing) is that the 40% entitlement itself is untouched and only the operational orders are paused. **Authoritative reading: the AGC's own statement plus the Court of Appeal's reasoning ("fait accompli" on payment magnitude) supports the second reading — only the calculation and payment timetable is stayed, not the constitutional entitlement.** Both sides' framings are quoted in cards.
- **"Lost years" span**: Some sources say 47 years (1974-2020 inclusive), others 48 (1974-2021 inclusive). The High Court judgment as reported in The Star (18 Oct 2025) uses 1974-2021. We use 48 years and 1974-2021 to match the court's framing.
- **Federal response to AKPS deferral**: As of dataset close, no federal-government public response to the 13 May Sabah Cabinet decision has been indexed across our 33 sources. We treat federal silence as a factual condition, not an assumption of agreement.
