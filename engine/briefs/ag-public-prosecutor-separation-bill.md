# ISSUE 1997 — Eight Years Of Promises, Six PSSC Meetings, One "Best Compromise": Who Actually Loses Their Grip On Prosecution?

## ISSUE
On 18 May 2026, the Parliamentary Special Select Committee (PSSC)
chaired by Datuk Seri Azalina Othman Said held its sixth and final
working meeting on the **Constitution (Amendment) (No. 2) Bill 2026**,
which would split the offices of the Attorney-General and Public
Prosecutor for the first time in Malaysia's history. Azalina, the
Minister in the Prime Minister's Department for Law and Institutional
Reform, called it "history in the making" and announced the revised
bill would be re-tabled for First Reading on 22 June 2026. Mainstream
coverage framed the moment as bipartisan progress on a long-overdue
accountability reform.

The editorial question is who actually wins from the version that
emerged. **Article 145** of the Federal Constitution has combined the
two roles in a single executive-appointed officer since 1957. The
Pakatan Harapan 2018 manifesto pledged to separate them "immediately";
eight years and four Attorneys-General later — Tommy Thomas
(2018-2020), Idrus Harun (2020-2023), Ahmad Terrirudin (2023-2024),
Mohd Dusuki Mokhtar (since November 2024) — the reform has still not
passed. In February 2026, ten government PKR MPs led by Datuk Seri
Rafizi Ramli publicly threatened to block the original bill, arguing
its appointment mechanism still concentrated power in the Prime
Minister and the Judicial and Legal Service Commission (JLSC). The
"middle ground" Azalina now celebrates was reached *without* a
parliamentary veto, *without* a public confirmation hearing, and
*with* the seven-year fixed tenure intact. The Bar Council, in its
14 March 2026 motion, separately flagged that the bill does not
prohibit appointment of the AG from among MPs or Cabinet members.

The story is not whether the offices should be split — every serious
voice agrees they should. The story is what kind of split: a
Singapore-style enhanced independence, a UK/Australia-style
parliamentary-overseen DPP, or a Malaysian institutional shell that
preserves executive influence under a new name. The bill's
appointment route — JLSC recommendation, PM advice to the Yang
di-Pertuan Agong — keeps the dominant decision points exactly where
they have been.

## PERIOD
March 2018 (Pakatan Harapan manifesto pledge) through 18 May 2026
(PSSC sixth meeting, "best compromise" announcement), with key
inflection points at 9 May 2018 (PH government takes office),
28 February 2020 (Tommy Thomas resignation amid Sheraton Move),
27 February 2026 (Bill first reading in Dewan Rakyat), 3 March 2026
(referral to PSSC by bipartisan agreement), and 22 June 2026
(scheduled re-tabling for First Reading of revised bill).

## ACTORS

## RELEVANT LAW

**Malaysia**
- *Federal Constitution Article 145*: establishes the Attorney-
  General. Article 145(3) vests in the AG "power, exercisable at
  his discretion, to institute, conduct or discontinue any
  proceedings for an offence, other than proceedings before a
  Syariah court, a native court or a court-martial." Article 145(2)
  makes the AG the principal legal adviser to the Government.
  Article 145(1) provides the AG is appointed by the Yang
  di-Pertuan Agong on the advice of the Prime Minister.
  <https://www.asianparliament.org/uploads/Country/Members/malaysia/malaysia%20const.pdf>
- *Federal Constitution Article 138*: establishes the Judicial and
  Legal Service Commission.
- *Federal Constitution Article 159*: amendments to most provisions
  (including Article 145) require a two-thirds majority of the
  total number of members of each House of Parliament. Constitutional
  amendments are not subject to suspension by the Senate (Dewan
  Negara) — but the two-thirds threshold applies in both houses for
  most amendments.
- *Federal Constitution Article 42*: pardon power of the Yang
  di-Pertuan Agong, exercised on the advice of the Pardons Board
  (which includes the Attorney-General). The reform programme has
  proposed consequential amendments to Article 42.
- *Federal Constitution Article 183*: prosecution of public servants
  in respect of acts done in official capacity requires AG consent.
  Consequential amendments proposed.
- *Criminal Procedure Code* (Act 593): governs criminal procedure;
  Section 254 covers withdrawals (DNAA and full acquittal).
- *Constitution (Amendment) (No. 2) Bill 2026*: the bill before
  the PSSC. As originally tabled 27 February 2026, it (a) splits
  the AG and PP into two offices; (b) creates a fixed seven-year
  tenure for the PP; (c) routes PP appointment via JLSC
  recommendation to the Yang di-Pertuan Agong on the advice of the
  Prime Minister; (d) requires the PP candidate to be Malaysian
  with at least 10 years of litigation experience. The revised
  18 May 2026 version's exact text has not been publicly released
  at the time of brief drafting.

**Comparator jurisdictions**
- *Singapore Constitution Article 35(8)*: vests prosecutorial
  discretion in the Attorney-General; Singapore retains a *combined*
  AG-PP office but uses a "dual-key" appointment requiring both
  Presidential and Prime Ministerial concurrence, and the AG is a
  non-political constitutional officer. **Singapore did not solve
  the conflict by separating offices; it solved it by depoliticising
  appointment.**
  <https://www.mlaw.gov.sg/news/parliamentary-speeches/response-speech-by-senior-minister-of-state-for-law-edwin-tong-at-the-committee-of-supply-debate-2020-(attorney-general's-chambers)/>
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attorney-General_of_Singapore>
- *United Kingdom — Prosecution of Offences Act 1985*: created the
  Crown Prosecution Service and the modern Director of Public
  Prosecutions, following the 1981 report of the Royal Commission
  on Criminal Procedure chaired by Sir Cyril Philips. The Attorney
  General "superintends" the CPS but has "no influence over the
  conduct of prosecutions" except in national security matters and
  a small list of offences requiring AG consent.
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crown_Prosecution_Service>
  <https://www.cps.gov.uk/sites/default/files/documents/publications/Framework_agreement_between_the_Law_Officers_and_the_Director_of_Public_Prosecutions__CPS.pdf>
- *Australia — Director of Public Prosecutions Act 1983* (Cth):
  established the Commonwealth DPP, operational from 5 March 1984.
  CDPP is independent but sits within the Attorney-General's
  portfolio; under Section 8 the Attorney-General may issue
  directions only after consultation with the Director, and
  directions must be tabled in Parliament.
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Director_of_Public_Prosecutions_(Australia)>
  <https://www.cdpp.gov.au/about-us>
- *India — Constitution Article 76*: establishes the Attorney
  General for India. The Solicitor-General and Additional
  Solicitors-General are statutory positions under the *Law
  Officers (Conditions of Service) Rules 1987*, not constitutional.
  India operates a separate Directorate of Prosecution at state
  level under the Code of Criminal Procedure.

**International standards**
- *UN Guidelines on the Role of Prosecutors* (Havana, 1990) —
  paragraph 4: "States shall ensure that prosecutors are able to
  perform their professional functions without intimidation,
  hindrance, harassment, improper interference or unjustified
  exposure to civil, penal or other liability."
- *Bangalore Principles of Judicial Conduct* (2002) — applies to
  judges but extensively cited in Malaysian Bar submissions on
  prosecutorial independence.

## 12-DIMENSION RISK ASSESSMENT

1. **Sentiment** — MEDIUM: Story carries a natural anger-at-process
   register; the editorial line is anger at institutional design,
   not at any individual minister or party. Avoid the
   thinkpiece tone — the facts are concrete enough to carry
   themselves.
2. **Political** — HIGH: Critique implicates the *current
   government's* draft, but the deeper critique is structural and
   reaches back to every government since 2018. T4A's framing must
   make clear this is not opposition cheerleading — the original
   PH manifesto, the PN government, and the current Madani
   government have all delivered nothing for 8 years. The Rafizi
   dissent is from inside the governing coalition.
3. **Ethnic** — LOW: Not an ethnic narrative directly. The
   prosecutorial-discretion archive (Anwar, Lim Guan Eng, Daim,
   Najib) includes named Malay, Chinese, and Indian-Malaysian
   figures; cards should avoid implying ethnic patterning of
   discretion.
4. **Religious** — LOW-MEDIUM: Article 145(3) already excludes
   Syariah-court proceedings from AG discretion. The Zakir Naik
   non-prosecution archive is adjacent but not central; brief
   references it only as context. Cards should not foreground
   religious-charged cases.
5. **Royalty** — MEDIUM: The Yang di-Pertuan Agong's role in
   appointing the AG and (under the bill) the PP, and the
   Conference of Rulers' consultation role in JLSC chair
   appointment, are constitutionally central. Cards must describe
   royal functions factually as constitutional roles and never
   critique royal discretion.
6. **Narrative** — HIGH: Mainstream framings are "bipartisan
   progress" and "long-overdue reform". T4A's contribution is the
   structural-design question: separation alone does not solve
   the appointment-capture problem. The Rafizi-Bar Council axis is
   the under-reported angle.
7. **Completeness** — HIGH: The PSSC report and the revised bill
   text are **not publicly available** at brief-drafting. Cards
   must label this gap, not paraphrase the Minister's press
   remarks as if they were the bill. The "best compromise" claim
   cannot be independently verified against text.
8. **Temporal** — MEDIUM: The 22 June 2026 First Reading is the
   next concrete data point; cards must not imply the bill will
   pass.
9. **Confidence** — HIGH on the timeline of public events,
   PSSC composition, named statements, and AG turnover record.
   MEDIUM on bill-content specifics post-PSSC revision. LOW on
   any claim about what the PSSC actually negotiated in closed
   session.
10. **Geographic** — LOW: National story; PSSC meetings in
    Putrajaya.
11. **Economic** — LOW: Reform does not directly affect economic
    figures; indirect effects on investor perceptions of rule of
    law are real but speculative.
12. **Gender** — LOW: Not a gendered story. Azalina is the
    minister driving the reform but the substance is institutional.

## CONTRADICTIONS

- **Number of PSSC members** — Reported variously as "9 MPs"
  (The Star headline-line summary) and "12 MPs including
  Azalina-as-chair" (Edge listing all named members). The correct
  figure: 1 chair (Azalina) plus 11 named MPs = 12 voting members.
  The "9" figure in earlier coverage appears to refer to an
  initial proposed composition before opposition opt-in. Use
  "the 12-member Special Select Committee" in cards or "the
  Azalina-chaired PSSC" if the precise number is not
  load-bearing.

- **Date of Tommy Thomas resignation** — The Wikipedia entry and
  some news pieces cite "28 February 2020" (the date the Sheraton
  Move government took office); other accounts date the formal
  resignation slightly later. Use **28 February 2020** as the
  conventional date.

- **AG turnover count: 4 vs 5** — From 2018 to 2026: Thomas →
  Idrus → Terrirudin → Dusuki = **4 distinct office-holders**.
  Idrus's 6-month extension in March 2023 is a tenure extension,
  not a new appointment. Cards should say "four
  Attorneys-General" not five.

- **Original bill tenure: 7 years confirmed; "10 years"
  qualification confirmed** — These two figures are robust across
  Edge, FMT, and Malay Mail reporting. The dissenting PKR group
  asked for **4 years** tenure as the alternative.

- **"Bipartisan support" framing vs Bar Council and PKR dissent
  framing** — The Minister characterises the 18 May outcome as
  unanimous agreement of the committee. The Bar Council
  (14 March 2026 motion) and the ten PKR MPs (24 February 2026
  letter) had separately flagged structural concerns. The
  PSSC's report text has not been released. Cards should report
  the Minister's claim as a claim ("Azalina said") not as a
  fact, and note that the dissent voices were structurally
  outside the PSSC.

- **"Middle ground" framing** — Used by Azalina (16 April 2026
  FMT) and re-echoed in The Edge / FMT / Bernama coverage. The
  exact substance of the compromise (whether parliamentary
  confirmation hearing was added; whether tenure shortened) is
  **not specified in public reporting** at brief-drafting time.
  Cards must not characterise the compromise substance unless
  the PSSC report becomes public before publication. Label this
  as an unknown.

- **Comparator framing for Singapore** — Singapore does **not**
  operate a separated AG-PP model; its AG-PP remain combined,
  with depoliticised dual-key appointment. Some Malaysian
  commentary erroneously implies Singapore separated the
  offices. Cards must not list Singapore as a "split" comparator;
  Singapore is the *alternative* model (depoliticise without
  splitting).

## EDITORIAL FRAME (provisional, for Stage 1)

The mainstream framings sort along two predictable lines:

1. **"Eight-year wait finally ends — bipartisan reform."**
   Government / Azalina / The Star / The Edge front-page line.
   True at the level of process; obscures the question of *which
   model* and *who keeps influence*.
2. **"This is good but inadequate — Parliament still locked out."**
   Rafizi / Bar Council / IDEAS line. Sharp on the appointment
   route; loses the public because the technical objection
   sounds like factional sniping.

T4A's distinct contribution lives in the **gap between separation
and independence**. Splitting the office is necessary; it is not
sufficient. If the new PP's appointment route still runs through
the JLSC (whose chair is appointed on PM advice) and the AG
(executive-appointed politician under the unchanged Article 145
appointment route), then the institutional shell has changed but
the appointment grip has not. Singapore's lesson is the inverse —
keep the office combined but depoliticise the appointment, and the
substantive independence improves. Malaysia is proposing to keep
the appointment route intact and split the office. The Rafizi
dissent and the Bar Council motion are the only Malaysian voices
arguing this trade is the wrong one.

The reframe should rotate readers from "is the reform finally
happening?" to **"separation is the easy part — independence is
the part nobody has agreed to."**

Working WhatsApp-test view-card sentence (draft): *"Eight years of
promising to split the office. Six PSSC meetings to call it 'the
best compromise'. Zero changes to who picks the prosecutor."*

Alternative: *"Splitting the office without changing who picks the
prosecutor is renovation, not reform."*

## STAGE 1 GUIDANCE

The reader needs to leave with three things:
1. Separation of the AG and PP offices was promised in 2018 and
   is now within reach for the first time — that is real.
2. The version that emerged from the PSSC preserves the PM's
   line of influence over both appointments; the parliamentary
   veto and public confirmation that ten PKR MPs and the Bar
   Council asked for is **not** in the "best compromise".
3. The comparator lesson is that Singapore did not separate the
   offices and still has substantive independence, because
   appointment was depoliticised. Malaysia is doing the inverse
   — separating offices while leaving appointment politicised.

The reframe must be structural, not partisan. The view card must
pass the WhatsApp test — a single sentence a thoughtful reader
would paste into a group chat with no commentary. The
draft *"separating without changing who picks the prosecutor is
renovation, not reform"* is the working line; Stage 6 should sharpen
it further.

**Fact card lens-diversity check**: the three fact cards should
each carry a distinct lens. Suggested mapping:
- Fact 1 (lead — identifiable case): **Political** — the 10 PKR
  MP dissent, named individuals.
- Fact 2 (shocking number / arousal peak): **Governance** — eight
  years, four AGs, six PSSC meetings, zero changes to the
  appointment route.
- Fact 3 (missing voice / comparator): **Legal** — Singapore did
  not split the office and still has higher independence; the
  bill's appointment route through the JLSC keeps the PM's hand
  in the same place.
