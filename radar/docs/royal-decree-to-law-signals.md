## Part 1 — The constitutional mechanism (the "how")

A royal pronouncement, on its own, is not law. The Federal
Constitution sets the conversion path; the radar's role is to flag
when the political weight of a pronouncement gets close enough to the
legislative pipeline that the conversion is plausible.

**Five routes from royal will to binding law:**

1. **Article 66 — royal assent on federal bills.** Every bill passed
   by both Houses of Parliament must be presented to the Yang
   di-Pertuan Agong. After the 1984 constitutional amendment and the
   1994 follow-on, Article 66(4) and 66(4A) make assent automatic —
   if the Agong does not assent within 30 days, the bill becomes law
   regardless. Royal assent is now procedural, not substantive. The
   substantive royal influence lives upstream of this step (Cabinet
   consultation, audiences with the PM) and is not on the public
   record.

2. **State enactments.** Each state legislative assembly passes
   enactments that need the assent of the state Sultan / Yang di-Pertua
   Negeri. The thirty-day clock applies analogously in most state
   constitutions. State-level religious enactments (syariah
   procedure, fatwa enforcement, takaful, halal certification) flow
   through this route — the Sultan is head of Islam in the state
   (Article 3(2)) and the religious authority sits under the Sultan,
   not under the federal government.

3. **Article 38 — Conference of Rulers concurrence.** Amendments
   touching Articles 38, 70, 71 (status, succession, privileges of
   Rulers), Articles 152, 153 (national language, special position of
   Malays and Bumiputeras), and Article 3 (Islam as religion of the
   Federation) require the Conference's concurrence. The Conference
   also must be consulted on national policy on Islam, citizenship
   law affecting Malay/Islam status, judicial appointments at the
   Federal Court level, and changes to state borders. Conference
   communiqués are not law, but they are a precondition for any law
   that would alter these protected articles.

4. **Article 150 — emergency proclamations.** The Agong proclaims an
   emergency only on Cabinet advice (Article 40), and the Court of
   Appeal's 2026 ruling on the non-justiciability of past emergency
   proclamations (T4A issue 1988) clarified the judicial line on
   review. Ordinances issued under an emergency proclamation have
   the force of law without parliamentary passage, but only for the
   duration of the emergency.

5. **Sentencing — "at the Sultan's / Agong's pleasure".** Section 97
   of the Child Act 2001 (and predecessor provisions) directs that a
   convicted juvenile who would otherwise face capital punishment is
   detained during the pleasure of the Sultan (state) or the Agong
   (Federal Territories). The Sultan's discretion here is genuine
   discretion — not bound by Cabinet advice — and is the cleanest
   example of a royal decision functioning as live legal authority
   in present-day Malaysia. The Taiping juvenile case in the queue
   (#661 below) is exactly this provision in operation.

**What is *not* law:** Royal addresses at the opening of Parliament
(drafted by Cabinet), Conference of Rulers communiqués, individual
Sultans' titah at state events, palace press statements. These are
high-authority political signals — and on 3R-sensitive matters
their moral weight is disproportionate to their legal status — but
they do not bind unless the legislature converts them.

## Part 2 — Why the question is live now (the radar signals)

A coherent cluster has formed across the 2026-Q2 queue. Eleven
royal-tagged items, three of them at medium-or-higher controversy
score, point in one direction: the political space around royal
authority is being formalised into legal form, and existing royal
authorities are being publicly tested. The radar reads this as a
single arc, not a sequence of unrelated incidents.

### Cluster A — Royal authority being converted into new statute

**Signal #407 — proposed special Act on royal-institution insults**
(controversy 0.196, political dimension). Source: BH 2026-05-08.
Headline: "Gubal RUU khas tangani penghina institusi diraja,
kerajaan diberitahu." The proposal asks Parliament to enact a
dedicated statute against insults, *penderhakaan* (treason / lèse-
majesté), and provocation against royal institutions and the
position of Islam. This is the cleanest live example of the
mechanism in Part 1, Route 1 — a royal-adjacent grievance moving
from titah-and-palace-statement into draft federal legislation.

The radar treats this as the spine of the cluster. If it
proceeds, every other signal below feeds into it as case material:
the rally, the prosecutions, the DPM's call for "firm action".

**Signal #35 — DPM calls for firm action on insults to Islam and
Malay rulers** (controversy 0.494, ethnic + political + religious,
priority *medium*). Date: 2026-05-10. The framing pairs the royal
institution with the "position of Islam" — the same pairing in
signal #407's bill description. This is upstream lobbying: an
executive-branch signal that the special Act has political backing
at Cabinet level.

### Cluster B — Existing royal authority being publicly tested

**Signal #466 — Daulat Tuanku rally** (controversy 0.182). Five
thousand at Shah Alam in support of the royal institution and the
Sultan of Selangor, following the pig-farming controversy in the
state. This is the *political-mass* signal that gives the special
Act its constituency. A rally of this size on a royal-loyalty frame
is rare in the post-2008 era; the precedent T4A would compare to is
the 2009 Perak constitutional crisis rallies.

**Signal #235 — Selangor government demands MCMC act on online
insult to Sultan Sharafuddin** (controversy 0.318, April 2026). The
state government is directly invoking the federal regulator (MCMC,
CMA Section 233) against a private citizen's online speech. This
is the *existing-statute* version of what signal #407 wants to
codify more specifically.

**Signal #118 — Court of Appeal orders defence on offensive
messages to Raja Permaisuri Agong, 2023 case** (controversy 0.375,
legal dimension). April 2026. Three-year-old offence finally moving
to defence stage. This is the *judicial pipeline* showing that
existing prosecutions for royal-related speech are slow but
producing convictions or near-convictions in 2026.

### Cluster C — Royal authority operating as live law

**Signal #661 — Taiping pair detained at Sultan of Perak's pleasure**
(controversy 0.135). Court of Appeal rejected bail for two
convicted-as-teens defendants who killed the sister's brother.
Detention is at the Perak Sultan's pleasure under Child Act s.97.
This is the radar's reference instance for Part 1, Route 5 above —
a present-day case where royal discretion *is* the operative legal
mechanism, not an emblematic one.

**Signal #411 — Sultan of Pahang declares Kampung Bantal a "royal
village"** (controversy 0.196, May 17 2026). Designation appears
to be honorific / customary rather than statutory, but if it
attaches to gazetted land use or tourism funding it would be a
test case for state-prerogative re-codification. Verification needed
against the Pahang state gazette.

### Cluster D — Adjacent (lower confidence, watch list)

**Signal #138 — Mercy Malaysia backs MACC push for "royal charities
commission"** (controversy 0.366, ethnic + institutional). March 30.
Royal patronage of NGOs is a long-standing feature of Malaysian
civil society; the proposal to regulate it under a *royal* commission
name is the linguistic tell that this cluster is shaping a wider
register, not just a single bill. Worth keeping in the queue.

## Part 3 — Where this could go, editorially

This memo is not a publish recommendation by itself. Three pathways
the briefing author should weigh:

1. **Single issue on signal #407** (the special Act). The most
   T4A-shaped option: a concrete bill, a clear constitutional
   question (does it duplicate Sedition Act, CMA s.233, Penal Code
   s.504?), and an inflight pipeline to track. Highest 3R bar —
   the brief author must source the actual draft bill text, not
   characterisations.

2. **Wider arc piece** on how royal authority is being re-codified
   in 2026 — pulling clusters A, B, C together. Higher editorial
   leverage, but proportionately higher 3R risk. Would need to be
   written entirely as critique of *legislative process and statutory
   redundancy*, never as commentary on the institutions themselves.

3. **Defer**. The radar's signal strength is medium-high, not
   critical. If signal #407 produces a draft bill text within the
   next scan cycle, escalate. If not, the cluster keeps maturing
   and can be revisited when the bill actually reaches first reading
   in Parliament.

The Stage 5 escalation rule applies if any of these are developed:
the issue would mark 3R risk HIGH (Royalty + Religion both implicated
in the proposed Act's scope). Re-enable Stage 5 Grok Contrarian
for whichever path is taken — see `docs/adr/0004-retire-stages-4-and-5.md`.

Primary sources the brief must open and cite, page or section level,
before any card is written:

  full text from the AGC reprint, not summary.

  s. 504. The redundancy question for signal #407 is decided here.

  reference instance.

  insults-bill push has Conference backing or only Cabinet backing.
- The draft RUU itself (when tabled) or the Cabinet paper that
  authorised the drafting instruction.
- News spectrum: BH (signal #407 source), Malaysiakini, FMT, MalayMail,
  The Edge, plus at least one English-language opposition-aligned
  outlet and one Sabah/Sarawak voice for federal-vs-state dimension.

The CONTRADICTIONS section of the brief will likely surface around
*scope* — palace statements vs Cabinet statements vs Conference of
Rulers statements often diverge on whether a royal-protection statute
is "needed" or already "covered" by existing law.
